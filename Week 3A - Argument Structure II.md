## Missing Premises
- found from logical gaps in an argument
- usually not mentioned due to not being worth mentioning, but sometimes may be because they are controversial
	- *the government should privatise the water corporation, because the state, needs an industry which can act quickly to climate change* implies state-controlled industries cannot act quickly and flexibly
- the missing premise should be replaced by the *most plausible premise*
	- be charitable!
- the all-purpose missing premise
	- *in capitalist economies the means of production are privately owned, so capitalist states are inherently anti-democratic* might be tempted to add a missing premise that directly correlates the two statements, despite being redundant. (tautology)
	- also useless as constructing a standard argument seeks not to *evaluate* the argument, simply state it in clearer terms.
- a premise should ensure the primary words stay the same (don't switch end of causation for end of a noun)
- moral **be sparing to use them**
## Revised Standard Form
- RSF builds upon the steps already present in SF
- we wish to connect which premise connect to which conclusion
	- first remove inference indicators
	- add what grounds and use each statement is for and how the premises support the statement
- two types of arguments derived from two different arguments
	- convergent: the two statements independently support the premise irrespective of each other
	- linked: both statements support the conclusion together dependently.
