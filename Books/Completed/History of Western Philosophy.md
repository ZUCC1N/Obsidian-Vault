#### 2 The Milesian School
[[Thales]] was a native of Miletus, in Anatolia, a flourishing commercial city, in which there was a large slave population, and a class struggle between the rich and poor. Similar conditions prevailed in most of the cities of Anatolia at the time of Thales. like other cities of Ionia, Miletus saw important developments during the seventh and sixth centuries BCE. political power belonged to land owners, but this was gradually replaced by a plutocracy of merchants. They, were replaced by a tyrant, who achieved power by the support of the democratic party. As to putting a date to Thales, the best evidence is that he predicted an eclipse which must have taken place in 585 BCE. Thales is said to have travelled in Egypt, and bringing to the Greeks geometry. He seems to have discovered how to calculate the distance of a ship at sea from observations taken at two points on land, and how to estimate the height of a pyramid from the length of its shadow.

According to [[Aristotle]], he thought that water is the [[arche|Original Substance]], and that the earth rests on water. Aristotle also says he stated magnets have a soul, since it moves iron, but also, that all things are full of gods. not much is known of Thales, these above facts being the basis of definite consensus on him. Some of the stories are pleasant, such as Aristotle in *Politics*:
<p style="text-align: center;"><i>
'He was reproached for his poverty, which was supposed to show that philosophy is of no use. According to the story, he knew by his skill in the stars while it was yet winter that there would be a great harvest of olives in the coming year; so, having a little money, he gave deposits for the use of all the olive-presses in Chios and Miletus, which he hired at a low price because no one bid against him. When the harvest time came, and many were wanted all at once and of a sudden, he let them out at any rate which he pleased, and made a quantity of money. Thus he showed the world that philosophers can easily be rich if they like, but that their ambition is of another sort.<br>

</i></p>

The speculations of Thales, Anaximander, and Anaximenes are to be regarded as scientific hypotheses, and seldom show any undue intrusion of anthropomorphic desires and moral ideas.
#### 3 Pythagoras
[[Pythagoras]] was intellectually one of the most important men that ever lived, both when he was wise and when he was unwise. Mathematical logic begins with him, but with a mystical slant. He was a native of the island of Samos, Some say he was the son of a substantial citizen named Mnesarchos, others that he was the son of the god Apollo. In his time Samos was ruled by the tyrant Polycrates, who had a vast navy. Pythagoras disliked his government so left Samos. It is said Pythagoras visited Egypt, and learnt much of his wisdom there, but ultimately settled in Croton, southern Italy. there Pythagoras founded a society of disciples, which for a time had influence before the citizens turned against them. Pythagoras and his disciples then moved to Metapontion, where he died. in death he was credited with miracles and magic powers, but also the founder of a school of mathematicians. These two schools fought over the specifics of his memory (which from what we learn, you can guess who won).

#### 4 Heraclitus
Heraclitus was a mystic, but of a peculiar kind. He regarded fire as the fundamental substance; everything, like flame in a fire, is born by the death of something else. There is unity in the world, but it is a unity formed by the combination of opposites. he believe humans to be flawed creatures, who only could be persuaded by force to act for their own good. in this he believed in war, as he saw it as the point of life and simply a matter of justice and balance. He regards the soul as a mixture of fire and water, the fire being noble and the water lowly. he took that pleasure induced moisture in the soul, and that true enlightenment comes from a dry soul. he thought the world to be in perpetual flux, it always becoming something but never being anything.

#### 9 The Atomists
The founders of atomism were Leucippus and Democritus. Leucippus came from Miletus, and carried on the scientific rationalist tradition of the city, being greatly influenced by Parmenides and Zeno. Democritus was a native of Abdera in Thrace. He travelled widely in southern and eastern lands, perhaps having spent a considerable time in Egypt, and certainly visited Persia. Democritus was a contemporary of Socrates and the Sophists, and thus chronologically comes later, but it is also hard to separate from Leucippus. On the grounds of investigating the development of thought, it serves better to discuss him now. even then, his contemporaries largely ignored him, besides Aristotle due to his kinship as an Ionian.

Leucippus was led to atomism in the attempt to mediate between monism and pluralism. They believed that everything is made of atoms, which are physically indivisible; that between the atoms there is empty space; are impossible to destroy; are perpetually moving; and are infinite in number, shape and size. they also purportedly said types of atems differ in heat, the spherical atoms, making fire, being the hottest. atoms moved randomly around, like dust in sunlight. As a result of collisions, atoms collected together into things. despite the inherent chaos of such a theory, the atomists were staunch determinists. something set the world in motion, but also that action itself must have been caused by something else too, which is a very modern notion. 

unlike their contemporaries the atomists wished to explain the world without the notion of fate or purpose. the distinction can be seen with the example of say a baker making bread. there is two different answers as to "why" the bread was baked, one that relies on purpose (to feed people) or one that relies on mechanics (the fermentation of yeast due to exposure to the conditions to make bread). the atomists unlike say Plato, wished to define the world by the second type opposed to the first. an important caveat to this is these questions and explanations could only be based in microcosms opposed to macrocosms as it introduces some circular logic which doesn't aid in answering anything within reality. even then, we may only view the atomists with some credibility in their theory only because *their theory was right*, as at the time any theory was being based solely on the observations of logical ability of the time. its a confirmation bias that we think them to be ahead of their time, for if they were incorrect we would view them very differently, perhaps in the same light as some aspects of Parmenides and Empedocles doctrine.

#### 22 Aristotle's Logic
Aristotle's greatest contribution to philosophy over time was logic, for a time it was metaphysics but the renaissance ended that. even to this day all catholic teachers of philosophy refuse to accept modern logic and opt for Aristotle's model. his model was a large step but it's hard to acknowledge his work when in the end his logic was a good model but was marked as stagnation for 2 millennia, and in the end was a dead end. for many of his predecessor's its easy to speak of them and praise and demerit their work. Aristotle's logic is still a battle ground, making it far harder. his most important work was the doctrine of the syllogism. a syllogism is an argument that consists of 3 parts, a major premise, a minor premise, and a conclusion. there are many different types, the most common the "Barbara":

All men → mortal (Major premiss).
Socrates → a man (Minor premiss).
Therefore: Socrates → mortal (Conclusion).

Or:

all men → mortal.
All Greeks → men.
Therefore: All Greeks → mortal.

Other forms are: 

Celarent:
No fishes → rational.
All sharks → fishes.
Therefore: No sharks → rational.

Darii:
All men → rational.
Some animals → men.
Therefore: Some animals → rational.

Ferio:
No Greeks → black.
Some men → Greeks.
Therefore: Some men → not black.

These four make up the 'first figure'; Aristotle adds a second and third figure, and the schoolmen added a fourth. It is shown that the three later figures can be reduced to the first by various methods.

There are some inferences that can be made from a single premiss. From 'some men → mortal' we can infer that 'some mortals → men'. Aristotle say this can be also inferred from 'all men → mortal', which seems a little loose in usage. From 'no gods → mortal' we can infer 'no mortals → gods', but from 'some men → not Greeks' it does not follow that 'some Greeks → not men'. in short, sometimes a → b does imply b → a but not always, entirely based on circumstances. besides this Aristotle thought all logic could be put in forms of syllogisms and thus avoid all fallacies. the method can be primary criticised in 3 ways.
##### Formal defects within the system itself.
Let us begin with the two statements 'Socrates is → man' and 'all Greeks → men'. these are diffrent statements, but not in Aristotelian logic. The statement 'all Greeks → men' needs the implication Greeks exist, as without it some syllogisms are invalid. for example, 'All Greeks → men, All Greeks → white, therefore some men → white.' If I were to say, 'All golden mountains → mountains, all golden mountains → golden, therefore some mountains → golden,' my conclusion would be false even as a minimum despite both premises being true. If we are to be explicit, the statement 'all Greeks → men' has to be divided into 'there → Greeks', and 'if anything is a Greek, it → a man'. the second is hypothetical and does not imply Greeks do exist. 'all Greeks → men' is a much more complex statement than 'Socrates is → man'. 'Socrates is → man' has 'Socrates' for its subject, but 'all Greeks → men' does not have 'all Greeks' for its subject, since the correct form of logical statements that describe it does not mention it.

this error in formality was a source of error in both metaphysics and the theory of knowledge. consider what is known from the statements 'Socrates → mortal' and 'all men → mortal'. In order to know the truth of 'Socrates → mortal', there needs to be some anecdotal account of Socrates, but for that to be reliable, we have to trace us back to someone that knew Socrates and saw him dead. the dead body of Socrates identified as being Socrates confirms his mortality. But when it comes to 'all men → mortal', the matter is different. have we seen every man ever to live dead? we are fairly certain as no man seems to have lived longer than say 150 years, but we are not entirely sure. others are purely implied, 'all Greeks → men' is known as a Greek is a man, it can be told from the dictionary and only defines word usage and nothing of the world. Metaphysical errors arose through supposing that 'all men' being the subject of 'all men → mortal' is analogous to 'Socrates' being the subject of 'Socrates → mortal'. It made it possible to hold that, in some sense, 'all men'  is the same king of entity as 'Socrates'. This led Aristotle to say that in a sense a species is a substance. He is careful with this statement, but his followers, especially Porphyry, showed less caution.

Another error into which Aristotle falls through this mistake is to think that a predicate of a predicate can be a predicate of the original subject. If I say 'Socrates → Greek, all Greeks → human', Aristotle thinks this means 'human' is a predicate of 'Greek', and 'Greek' is a predicate of 'Socrates',  'human' is a predicate of 'Socrates'. But 'human' is not a predicate of 'Greek'. this makes Aristotle's metaphysical language blurred, with disastrous consequences to philosophy. for example it allows for a class with 1 member to be the same as the 1 member itself, making 1 an impossible number and leading to poor metaphysics on unity.
##### Over-estimation of the syllogism, as compared to other forms of deductive argument.
the syllogism is only 1 type of deductive argument, and in the grand scheme of mathematics is rare. you can write other arguments in syllogistic form but it would provide no discernible advantages. arithmetic for example seems rather nonsensical to even try to express in this form. and some statements are not syllogistic in form at all such as, 'A horse → an animal, therefore a horse's head → an animal's head.' Valid syllogisms are only a subset of valid deductions and are no greater than others, Kant viewing mathematics as not syllogistic, inferred that it must use some extra-logical principles to do this, meaning Aristotle lead him and his predecessor's astray.
##### Over-estimation of deduction as a form of argument.
the Greeks largely overemphasised the importance of deduction for knowledge compared to modern thinkers. Aristotle did this less than Plato, saying induction also had importance and also pondered what premises deduction should start from, but still overemphasised deductions powers. when we say all men are mortal, we aren't actually saying we know they are, but that "all men born after the oldest person are mortal, and so are almost all men born before them". this is an induction, not a deduction, as it only offers a probable statement, not a certain one. but it also offers new knowledge, while deductions do not. all inferences outside logic and pure mathematics are inductive, besides in laws and theology that draw first principles from unquestionable texts or scripture. in the Categories Aristotle does establish the theory of categories, but Russell admits he largely doesn't understand how categories are important to philosophy. Aristotle proposed a list of 10 categories and saying they are the components that form composite arguments, but offers no reasoning for how the categories are devised. his theory of substance applying to species and genus and not just things making things allows for much poor metaphysics. he defines the category of essence as a thing that constitutes a thing's essential nature. it's a rather muddled notion left to interpretation. it doesn't denote in practice the essence of a thing, but the group of terms that without them we do not call an object that thing. it is linguistic not physical.

people can become ill, but being ill is a process. everything has some process occurring to them and there is no solidness to their being that constitutes some essentialness to them. substances are also defined as being subject of properties, but distinct from the properties. but if you extract the properties of a thing you find its substance is left without anything to it. in essence this means 2 substances in his theory should be indistinguishable so is a useless distinction. substance also turns out to be a linguistic convivence, it groups things together. the group "France" is not an intrinsic thing but just a grouping of the physical land that makes up it's borders. a person's substance is just a group of their characteristics.

these points largely discredit Aristotle doctrines presented here, either false or unimportant in the grand scheme in the case of syllogisms.
#### 23 Aristotle's Physics

#### 13 St Thomas Aquinas
Saint Thomas Aquinas' most important work is *Summa contra Gentiles* was written during 1259 CE and 1264 CE, concerning the truth of Christian religion by arguments directed by a gentile, usually a man versed in the philosophy of the Arabs. in it he identifies wisdom to be in an Aristotelian sense concerned with the end of the universe, he also says that reason can prove god's existence and immortality of the soul, but not the trinity or incarnation. reason and revelation agree in earnest. so to encompass a gentile friendly doctrine of Christianity, the first 3 of 4 books make no appeals to revelation and only discuss conclusions drawn from reason, while the 4th book delves into the matters of revelation. Aquinas rejects the ontological argument on the basis that we know not god's essence but an imperfect ones, so cannot derive existence from that essence. he instead refers to Aristotle's principle of the unmoved mover to reason god's existence as a first cause. he further expands his proof in the *summa Theologiae* to include there has to be a perfection for things to contain the perfect they do and that everything has purpose, so god must be the purpose of non livings thins that cannot find purpose within themselves.

now that god is proven to exist, we can move on to the things about him. he is known to us through what he is not, he is eternal and unmoved, he does not change. he is not primary eternal matter but eternal activity. he has no body as he has no compositions or parts and is whole. he is his own essence, as that would make him complex with an essence and existence. he knows all. many things are like god while at the same time not. they differ from god in the attributes of his they do not have. he is goodness, his own goodness and itself the good of every good. he understands all but they have no distinct being to them separated from their own matter. nonetheless he has always understood their forms. in essence, god is truth. but the question is raised if god knows specific instance, or only the universals and general truths they follow the shape of and exert? Saint Thomas Aquinas points out 7 arguments against the principle he understand specific things before refuting them:

1. Singularity being distinct matter, nothing immaterial can know it.
2. Singulars once did not exist, and could not have been known then, thus they are not known by the unchanging god.
3. Singulars exist by chance, thus no certain knowledge of them exists except when they exist.
4. Some singulars are due to desire, which can only be known to the person willing.
5. Singulars are infinite in number, and the infinite as such is unknown.
6. Singulars are too petty for God's attention.
7. In some singulars there is evil, but God cannot know evil.

Aquinas retorts that god knows the cause of things, and knows things that do not exist yet. he sees everything that could exist as if it already does due to being external time. he understands everyone's mind and desires, and knows the infante expanse of all things, even the trivial as nothing is wholly trivial. he knows all good as to know it it requires to know what is not good. his will is hi essence, in his will he wills other things to do so, as god is the end of all things. but he does not will them necessarily all the time, giving rise to free will. reason is assigned to his will but not cause. he also cannot will impossible things, humans will never fly and truth will never be falsehood. god is delight and joy and love, the hates nothing. he is happy as he is happiness itself.

he then moves to discussion of creatures and things. god created out of nothing, and he cannot exhibit the folly of morals. he has no body, no change, does not fail, become weary, forget, repent, anger or despair. he cannot make a man have no soul. he cannot undo the past, sin, or make other gods. intellectual substance is immaterial and incorruptible. the soul permeates the body. animal souls are mortal while human souls are immortal. the intellect is part of the soul and it is not as Averroes maintained that we partake in a shared intellect. souls are created a new upon birth (ignoring the established fact the soul must have some connection to the parents through genes or such to transmit the original sin). his view of universals is Aristotelian; universals require man to understand them but by intellect understanding them they understand such that exists outside the soul.

the 3rd book moves to ethical questions. evil is without essence and accidental which is good. happiness does not consist in the vices of man, and is not seated in sense. happiness is only found in the contemplation of god, knowledge of god that transcends that of the majority or obtained through demonstration or faith. we do not have it in life, but only in death when we are brough before him, in a heaven outside of time. he rejects astrology, discouraging discussions of fate. god sometimes works miracles but nothing else does, however magic is possible with the help of demons. divine law directs love of god, to a lesser degree our neighbour. his theory of predestination and election is Augustinian in doctrine.

in the end his philosophy is Aristotelian in principle, but lacks vigour or goes much beyond it. he seeked through it justification of an end he already knew and wished to mould it to, making it lifeless at best.

Erasmus was born in Rotterdam. from a young age he was forced into being a monk and in 1493 CE, became secretary to the bishop of Cambrai allowing for him to travel. he travelled to the university of Paris to a time, but found nothing of profit there. the golden age of the university was over, the Thomists and Scotists in bitter feud with Occamists. at last in 1482 CE they reconciled and joined together against the humanists. Erasmus hated scholastics thinking them antiquated, but also did not really like any philosophy, but gave them respect for their age. in 1499 CE he first visited England, making friends with Colet and More, who encourage him to undertake serious work. using his knowledge of Greek, he decided to work on the bible, leaving England in 1500 CE setting work on refining his Greek. by 1502 CE he was proficient and in 1506 CE went to Italy. he was determined to edit Saint Jerome and bring out a Greek testament with a new Latin translation, both finished in 1516 CE. when the Protestants used the thus noticed inaccuracies in the vulgate to their advantage, he attempted to learn Hebrew but gave up. during 1509 CE and 114 CE he returned to England, spending his time partly in London, partly in Cambridge, stimulating English humanism.

Thomas More was much more admirable than Erasmus, but had less influence. he was a humanist and had profound piety. at oxford he set out to learn Greek, which lead to the idea he sympathised with Italian infidels. this lead to him being removed from the university, then being attracted to the Carthusians, practised extreme austerity, and contemplating joining the order. Erasmus deterred him. his father was a lawyer, More following him in profession. in 1504 CE he became a Member of Parliament and lead the opposition to Henry VII's demand for new taxes. he was successful but the king furious, sending More's father to the tower and demanding bond of .£100.  on the king's death in 1509 CE More returned to practicing lore and won favour of Henry VIII. he was knighted in 1514 CE and employed on various embassies. when Wolsey fell, the king appointed More as chancellor in his stead. he refused all gifts from the litigants and soon fell into disfavour due to Henry wishing to divorce Catherine to marry Anne, More being opposed to the divorce. he resigned in 1532 CE. in 1534 CE Henry pass the Act of Supremacy making him the head of the Church of England, More feuded to take an Oath of Supremacy and was imprisoned on treason. this did not incur the death penalty but upon dubious testimony that he said the parliament could not make Henry head of the church, they beheaded them and his property given to princess Elizabeth.

he is purely remembered for his account of his *Utopia*. it is an island in the southern hemisphere where everything is done in the best possible way, being accidentally visited by a sailor who spent 5 years there, only returning to Europe to spread the word of it's wiseness. as in Plato's *Republic*, all things are held in common, as the public cannot flourish with private property and without communism there is no equality. More objects that communism would make the men idle and destroy respect for magistrates, which the sailor replies that no man who has lived in Utopia would say this. the island consists of 54 towns, all on the same plan except for the capital. all streets are 20 feet wide, all housing the same, with 1 door onto the street and another into the garden. there are no locks as everyone may enter any house. roofs are flat. every 10 years everyone changes houses to prevent feelings of ownership. there are farms, containing no few than 40 people, including 2 bondmen. each farm is under a master and mistress, old and wise. chickens are hatched by incubator. all men dress alike and all women dress alike, and the wardrobe stays constant throughout the year. at work leather or skins are worn and a suit lasts 7 years. when they stop work for they day, they throw a cloak over the working clothes, the same cloak as anyone else's. each family makes their own clothes. everyone works 6 hours a day, 3 hours before dinner. all go to bed at 8pm, and sleep for 8 hours. the early morning has lectures, but are no compulsory. after dinner an hour is devoted to play. 6 hours of work suffices as there are no idlers, implying the existence of the rich leads to the production of unnecessary luxuries. if a surplus is found, a shorter work day for a time is mandated. some men are elected to be learnt men, being exempted from other work while occupied by this. all who govern are chosen from them, and the government is a representative democracy with a system of indirect election. a prince at the head is elected for life, unless he is overthrown.

families are patriarchal, sons live in their father's house even if married, being governed by him. if families grow too large, the extra children are moved to another family. if a town growths too large, they are redistributed and if all towns are too large, a new is built on waste land. nothing mentions what occurs when all the waste land is occupied. slaying of animals is done by bondmen to free citizens of cruelty. hospitals exist for the sick, sufficient that the ill seek them. eating is allowed at home, but most eat in common halls. the 'vile service' is done by bondmen but cooking is done by women and waiting by the older children. men sit at one bench, women at the others, mothers with children under 5 also separate. all women nurse their own children, and if too young to wait, they stand by in silence while the elders eat. everyone eats the same food. regarding marriage, both men and women are punished for lack of chastity when marrying, and any owner of a home where misconduct occurred are liable to occur infamy for such carelessness. before marrying, bride and groom see each other naked. there is divorce for adultery or 'intolerable waywardness' of either party, and the guilty cannot remarry. sometimes divorce can occur if both desire it, but breakers of wedlock are punished to bondage. iron is achieved through trade due to the island lacking it and purposes of war. they do not take glory in the military but all people learn how to fight, men and women. war occurs for 3 reasons:

1. to defend their territory
2. to deliver territory to an ally from invaders
3. to free an oppressed nation from tyranny

but if they can, they will employ mercenaries to fight for them. they aim to make other nations be in debt to them, and allow them to repay them by giving them mercenaries. there is no currency for them, and they teach contempt for gold by only using it for chamber pots and chains of bondmen. infants use pearls and diamonds, but never for adults. when at war, they offer large rewards for anyone to kill the prince of the enemy, and larger if they can capture him alive, or even to him if he surrenders willingly. they pity the Populus among their enemies. regarding ethics, they find joy in leisure. but they think in the next life the good are rewarded and wicked punished. they are not ascetic and consider it silly. they have many religions, and tolerate them all, almost all believing in God and immortality, the few who do not are considered not citizens having no part in political life. but otherwise left alone. some holy men are afforded their practice, but are considered holy not wise. women can be priests if old and widowed. priests are few, are respected but given no power. bondmen are condemned for heinous offences or foreigners who were condemned in death in their own countries and fleeing to Utopia. for incurable disease, euthanasia is advised, but tended to if they would rather not. the Sailor could apparently convert Utopians to Christianity when told Jesus was opposed to private property.
#### 7 Francis Bacon
Francis Bacon was born son of Sir Nicholas bacon, Lord Keeper of the Great Seal, growing up in the atmosphere of State affairs. he entered Parliament at 23, and became adviser to Essex before abandoning his post. in 1617 CE he gain his father's office and following year became Lord Chancellor, but after 2 years was prosecuted for accepting bribes from litigants. he willingly admitted to it, but said it never swayed his decision. he was sentenced to a fine of £40,000 and imprisoned in the tower of London and banished from office. he did not pay the fine and only spent 4 days imprisoned, but abandoned his public life and instead spent his life writing. after 5 years he died from hypothermia experimenting with refrigeration.

his most important work *The Advancement of Learning* demonstrates a remarkably modern thesis. Bacon is regarded as the originator of the phrase "Knowledge is power", and although others had said it before he said it with new emphasis. his entire philosophy was practical, seeking to tame nature for scientific discovery and inventions. he was an orthodox Christian but thought philosophy and theology should be separated, thinking reason could prove god's existence but any other religious truth was pure dogma and revelation. his philosophy should only depend on reason, believing the doctrine of "double truth" of reason and revelation. he was the first of a long line of scientific philosophers who emphasised induction over deduction. he seeked a better form of induction than "induction by simple enumeration" where upon seeing a trend simply assuming it follows for unobserved points. Bacon devised a hypothesis if you created groups of things with a certain property, things without it and somewhere in-between, you could induce it's nature by finding the common factors shared and not shared between things. he thought this could be used to find general laws which could be expanded together when combined and tested against unseen circumstances. he despised syllogisms, undervalued maths and objected to teleological explanations when investigating phenomena. his most famous doctrine was the enumeration of what he dubs "idols", being bad habits that cause error. they are:

- Idols of the tribe: inherent to human nature, he specifically points out our habit of expecting orderly trends in nature.
- Idols of the cave: personal prejudice.
- Idols of the market-place: errors induced by persuasion.
- Idols of the theatre: errors implicit to accepted systems of thought taken at face value.

despite being renowned for scientific vigour, he rejected the Copernican theory despite Kepler presenting sufficient evidence. his method had fault in not emphasising hypothesis. his hope that data being ordered making the right hypothesis fall out rarely was the case, which is disastrous with how important a hypothesis is. deduction is also more important than Bacon wished to give credit, due to how some observation must be devised via deduction no matter the hypothesis. he was right in rejecting simple enumeration, but he found no true solution to it, and to this day it remains a problem we must contend with.
#### 8 Hobbes' Leviathan
Hobbes was an Empiricist, but unlike his fellow empiricists he had a respect for pure mathematics and it's applications. his outlook was more Galilean than Bacon. From Descartes to Kant the continental philosophy took much from mathematics to conceive it's ideas of the nature of human knowledge, but separated it from the concept of experience, in essence minimising the involvement of abstract thought. English empiricism on the other was not influenced by maths and thus tended to have a faulty scientific method. Hobbes escaped this. but this doesn't make him perfect, he was impatient with smaller details and tended to over simplify things to his detriment. his solutions are logical but underlined by omitting awkward counter point. never the less his theory of state is worth considering, more so due to being perhaps the most modern theory we have. he was taught of Aristotle at Oxford at age 15, but in later life said he profited little from these years. working as a tutor for Lord Hardwick he travelled and gained knowledge of the work of Galileo and Kepler, who greatly influenced him. after the Lord's death, Hobbes lived for a time in Paris, studying Euclid, then travelled to Italy with the Lord's son visiting Galileo in 1636 CE and returning to England the following year.

the political opinions that Hobbes expresses in the *Leviathan* are extremely Royalist, Hobbes having held them for a long time. he opposed democracy and when the Long Parliament met in 1640 CE Hobbes fled to France .*De Cive* written the following year sets forth in essence the same theory of the *Leviathan*, demonstrating the English civil war did not create his opinions but rather strengthen them when his fears became reality. he become associated with figures such as Descartes at the time and welcomed English Royalist refugees. but in 1651 CE when he published the leviathan he pleased no one with it. the rationalism offended the refugees, it's attacks of the Church offended the French Government. Hobbes thus secretly fled to London and submitted to Cromwell, abstaining from political activity. he gain favour with the king's and his friends, to the extent the king awarded him a pension shocking the Lord Chancellor and parliament that such favour would be shown to a suspected atheist. after the plague and great fire, parliament appointed a committee to investigate atheistic writings, singling out Hobbes. from this point forward his publishing power was leashed and he was forbidden to leave England to print anything controversial. even his history of the Long parliament, *Behemoth* which was incredibly orthodox had to be published over sees.

In the *Leviathan* Hobbes begins by preaching his materialism. he considers life only a motion of limbs, giving automata artificial life. he calls the commonwealth the leviathan, being a creation of art and itself an artificial man. this is more analogy that literal. he says the sovereignty is akin to it's soul, and that the pact which created this Leviathan was created with god's proclamation "Let Us make man". he moves to the individual, outlining his theory of sense. he applies the first law of motion to psychology, calling imagination decaying sense, imagination dreaming when sleeping, religion being the undistinguishing of dreams from waking life. he disavows such delusions such as witchcraft or ghosts. Hobbes is a nominalist, thinking universals are but names and that without language there would be no true as truth and falsehood are only figures of speech. he thinks geometry is the only true science so far. reasoning is a type of reckoning, and should start from definitions, but you must avoid definitions that are self-contradictory which is seemingly usually ignored in philosophy. he thinks "incorporeal substance" to be a nonsensical statement. on that note he refutes the argument god to be incorporeal that god is not a subject of philosophy, and that many philosophers have considered him to be corporal. all error in general propositions come from such absurdities, including free will.

he next considers passions. Endeavours are defined as the beginning of motion. if towards something it is desire and if away aversion. love is the same as desire, hate the same as aversion. we desire good things and are averse to 'bad' things, which does nothing to define what are good or bad and in fact makes it nonsensical to suggest objective good and bad exists. he defines many passions, mostly around the competition of life such that laughter is sudden glory. what is publicly accepted fear of invisible power is religion, what is not allowed is superstition. thus the state ultimately decides what is to be believed and what is to be maligned. joy comes from progress and continually prospering. will is nothing but the final desire or aversion in deliberation, it's no different to them but strongest in conflict. unlike other defenders of despotic methods, Hobbes considered all equal. without government in nature we all desire personal liberty but to dominate others, both dictated by self-preservation. from this conflict arises war of all against all, which degrades life. nature has no property or justice only conflict. Hobbes says to escape this we must combine into communities referring each to a central authority. he says this happen via the social contract. people come together and choose a monarch to end the war, him meaning it in more a mythological sense than a historical fact  to explain the phenomena why men people choose to submit, should submit and the freedoms that come with this submission.. Hobbes believes the restraint's people choose to put on comes form the self-preservation from the war that would result if otherwise. the contract is not signed with the monarch itself but all the other subjects to agree to all follow them. with this choice their political power ends and their rights are only those the state find it expedient to grant. they are not given the right of rebellion as the king never signed such a contract. the multitude united is called a commonwealth, but this "Leviathan" created by the contract is a mortal god.

Hobbes may prefer monarchy, but his arguments apply to any body where one supreme authority is present not limited by the legal right of others. he could tolerate parliament, but not a system where the king and parliament share power. the civil war occurred to Hobbes due to division of power between kings, lords and commons. whoever is supreme he calls the Sovereign, who has unlimited power, having the power to censor all opinion. it's assumed he is benevolent to maintain peace, and therefore will be just in his use of censure, since a doctrine that opposes peace cannot be true. the sovereign should contain the laws of property as it's the state that secures property. the sovereign may be despotic, but that is still better than anarchy. also, in many ways the sovereign and people share common interest, he is richer if they are, safer if they obey the law and are safer. rebellion is wrong because it usually fails but also if it does succeed it teaches other rebelling is expedient. he considers tyranny only a monarch that an individual happens to dislike. he attributes criticisms of monarchy to also apply to assembly. an assembly can have vested interests, can play favourites, can hear advice in secret. assemblies can fluctuate quickly and thus produce change in policy, and if divided too much lead to civil war. to Hobbes, this means monarchy has inherent stability. the sovereign to Hobbes shall decide his successor irrespective of the people, and should be allowed to choose whoever he wishes.

there is a chapter on liberty, Hobbes saying liberty is the absence of an impedance of motion. man is natural free to his will but must necessarily act in God's will. as for subjects of the state, they are free where the laws do not interfere, the sovereign is above such laws. Hobbes considers that ancient authors love of liberty promotes a sense of rebellion. there is one right that Hobbes considers the sovereign unable to infringe on. all men shall have the right to self-preservation, so subjects have the right to defend themselves against monarch. this means that men have the right to refuse to fight when ordered by the state. Hobbes cites how easily people believe religion despite their nonsensical nature as to how it would be easy for people to agree with submission to such a sovereign.
#### 9 Descartes

#### 10 Spinoza
Spinoza is considered one of the most noble and lovable of the great philosophers, intellectually being surpassed by some but ethically he lays supreme. his chief work, *Ethics* displays this, but he has 2 other works of note. *Tractatus Theologico-Politicus* deals with a combination of biblical criticism and political theory, while *Tractatus Politicus* which deals only with the political theory. his biblical criticism largely deals in showing scripture is compatible with liberal theology. Spinoza's politics is derived from Hobbes despite their difference in temperance. but he disagrees in thinking democracy the "most natural" /neg form of government and that subjects should not sacrifice all rights to the sovereign. despite holding that religious questions should be decided by the state, he thinks freedom of opinion important. Spinoza's *Ethics* has 3 parts, a metaphysics, psychology on passions and will and an ethics based on the preceding. his metaphysic follows Descartes, the psychology Hobbes, but his ethics is distinct and of the most value. his relation to Descartes is in some ways like Plotinus to Plato. Descartes had many sides full of intellectual curiosity but also could be used by sceptics picking and choosing his doctrines akin to Carneades using Plato. Spinoza took from Descartes and his contemporaries material and deterministic physics, and within this framework seeked room for reverence and life devoted to good.

Spinoza's metaphysical system is of the type Parmenides held. there is 1 substance, being god, and nothing finite is self-subsistent. Descartes held 3 substances, god, mind and matter, but god was above the other 2 and could create or annihilate them. Spinoza thought they were only parts of god himself. god also has an infinite many other parts as he himself must be infinite, most are thus unknown to us. souls and matter are all just small parts of god. Christian personal immortality is but an extension of god's immortality and occurs by us becoming more and more one with god. in short, Spinoza is lead to undiluted pantheism in doctrine. an extension of this is that events occur in god's will. free will does not exist nor does chance, events cannot deviate from how they are. this ultimately leads to difficulty in sin, as it decrees that god wishes us to sin in the way we do. events such as Nero committing matricide or Adam eating the apple are implicitly directives of god's will. Spinoza retorts that the positives of these acts are good, and the negatives are bad, but this only works for finite creatures that can have flawed judgement. for god who is meant to be omnipotent he doesn't have this luxury of negligence, if his will ordains it to occur it should occur either perfectly or not at all.

his Ethics is set forth in the style of Euclid, with definitions, axioms and theorems, his arguments being deductively based on the axioms set forth. this makes reading it difficult, and for a more modern mind that has moved to accepting proofs of the things he wishes to prove don't exist, his demonstrations can be draining. thus it is enough to more study his points than how he exactly proves them. it must be admitted but that his system demanded he demonstrate it but, as it maintains that everything could be demonstrated so it would be negligent to not. his theory of emotions comes after a metaphysical discussion of the nature and origin of mind, leading to the proposition the human mind has adequate knowledge of the essence of god. but passions discussed in the 3rd book of *Ethics* distract our intellectual vision. we have the instinct of self preservation gives rise to love, hate and strife. but self preservation alters when we realise the best and most positive course of action is uniting to preserve us all, not our separateness. the last 2 books, entitled respectively ***Of human bondage, or the strength of the emotions*** and ***Of the power of the understanding, or of human freedom,*** are the most interesting. we are chained in proportion to outside causes, and free in proportion to our self-determination. Spinoza like Socrates and Plato thinks all wrong action is due to error, the person that understand his circumstances will act the best, and will be happy no matter the misfortune. he will act in self preservation but due to his intellect direct it to the best method of preservation, in gaining knowledge in line with god. pleasure is good, but hope, fear, humility and repentance bad, as Spinoza considers repentance a lack of confidence. Spinoza considers time unreal, so thus emotions that align with past or future are unreasonable. what is, simply is, all a part of god's timeless world. upon seeing the world in line with god's will you will see this, and as god is good, acting in contrary to it, that things are not a part of that god, is where evil is born.

Spinoza's outlook intends to free men from the tyranny fear. fear, especially fear of death paralyses us from true wisdom and meditation, and living our life with that life in mind. internal cause is always good, evil is only born from what comes outside of us. this means nothing evil can occur to the universe, as it has no external causes. we are part of universal nature, and we follow it's order. if we understand this, the part of us that is intelligent will acquiesce to our misfortunes to overcome it. as long as a man resists, he is in bondage, but through understanding this he becomes free. Spinoza like the stoics does not object to emotions, only to passions that come from outside and we are passive to. upon understanding it's course, and that all things are necessary, we gain control over emotions. this results in the "intellectual love of god", the combination of true thought and the joy of obtaining it. it contains nothing negative, so is part of the whole. he cannot hate god, but we must not endeavour for god to ever love us, as god is beyond emotions, love means nothing to him. love of god is not the reward of virtue, but the virtue itself, as we do not rejoice because we control our lusts, we control our lusts so thus rejoice in it.
#### 11 Leibniz
Leibniz was one of the highest intellects of the time, but not the most admirable human. he was an ideal employee, industrious, frugal, temperate and honest, but destitute of philosophic virtues. what he published was only so he could win favour with princes and princesses, his more interesting work left unpublished. this means there are 2 systems of Leibniz, one optimistic, orthodox, fantastic and shallow, while another which was profound, followed Spinoza and amazingly logical. he was the one that created the doctrine that this is the best possible world of all the combinations. he also discovered infinitesimal calculus around the same time as Newton, but due to his work going unpublished caused controversy on if one copied the other. his popular philosophy is put forth in *Monadology* and *Principles of Nature and of Grace*. 
##### Substances
Like Descartes and Spinoza, his philosophy is based on notions of substance, but differs in how many substances there are and the relation of mind and matter. Descartes had God, mind and matter, while Spinoza said God existed alone, both believing in extension of them. Leibniz thought extension not to be an attribute of substance, as it involved plurality and thus exists only in aggregation so cannot be a property of singular substance. this means there must be an infinite number of substances, each called "monads", each have the properties of a physical point, only when viewed abstractly, each monad being a soul in essence. the only remaining attribute beyond this must be thought. this means Leibniz rejects physical realty for a world of an infinite family of souls. the doctrine that substances do not interact was inherited from Descartes, no two monads being unable to have causal relation to each other, and if it seems they have this is a deception. he regards monads as "windowless, which leads to difficulties regarding collisions, and that it makes perception in general impossible, as there must be a relation between perceiver and percipient. he held that every monads mirrors, not because the universe affects it but because God has given it a nature which spontaneously produces the result. there is 'pre-established harmony' between changes in monads, producing the illusion of interaction, being an extension of Descartes' clocks, except Leibniz says they are in essence infinite in number. they are placed in a hierarchy that mirrors their hierarchy in the universe. there is in all some confusion of perception, but the amount varies depending on the dignity of the specific monad. a human body is made entirely of monads, each with their own soul, each immortal, but there is 1 supreme monad in them being the soul. changes in the body happen for the sake of this monad, when an arm moves the purpose of it is in the dominant monad, meaning it belongs to the mind not the arm. 
##### Free Will and God
he contrasts Spinoza in free will with his 'principle of sufficient reason' saying that all events have a reason, but when it comes to free will the reason "incline without necessitating", meaning there is a motive to the action but no logical necessity. God also follows this, but acts in the best path, and decrees the best course of logic to be as so. this is generally a part of his metaphysical proof of god's existence, following a long line of them. he critiques Descartes' revival of the ontological argument, stating it better than any before while also saying it cannot stand on it's own.

his arguments are as followed:
###### The Ontological Argument
Leibniz cites the ontological argument, created by [[History of Western Philosophy#9 Ecclesiastical Reform in the Eleventh Century|Saint Anslem]], neither accepting nor rejecting it, saying it needs to be supplemented. he writes a similar proof that he presented to Spinoza, saying that God is the most perfect Being, perfection being the simple quality that is positive and absolute, and expresses without limits whatever it wishes to express. he then easily proves that no two perfection under this definition can be incompatible, concluding that there must be a subject of all perfections, and must exist as one of those perfections. Kant countered this argument by saying "existence" is not a predicate.
###### The Cosmological Argument
the cosmological argument follows the form of the First-Cause argument, itself drawn from Aristotle's argument of the unmoved mover. Leibniz modifies this to stipulate the world is "contingent", meaning it's logically possible for it not to exist. if we suppose the universe has always existed, there cannot be anything within it to explain why it does, but Leibniz thought all things must have a sufficient cause, so there must be some reason outside the universe, that being god. this is a harder argument to refute compared to the First-Cause argument, as the First-Cause relies on the idea a series must have a first term, which is false. it remains true as longas you afford it Leibniz's principle of sufficient reason, but otherwise, it falls apart. it's ambiguous what he exactly meant, some maintaining that it means every proposition is "analytic". but Kant rightfully identifies that this argument is largely contingent on the ontological argument, as if the existence of the world is accounted for by the existence of some necessary thing, than that being must have an essence that involves existence. which largely collapses into an Ontological Argument.
###### Argument from eternal Truths
the Argument of eternal Truth states some statements such as "it is raining" are sometimes true, other times false, but statements like "2 + 2 = 4" are always true, or "2 + 2 = 5" is always false. statements that deal in only essence are either true or false and always that, called "eternal truths". these eternal truths are contents of mind, and such an eternal truth must be part of the eternal mind, and Leibniz contends that the reason for contingent truth must exist in necessary truths. this then follows the cosmological argument, that the reason for the contingent truth of the world must exist in this eternal truths, but reason for what exists must therefore exist in that eternal truth as well, meaning it exists in what is the eternal mind of god. this thus has similar problems as the Cosmological argument, as well as the critique it's hardly apt to say a mind that apprehends an idea also has that idea "exist" in it.
###### Argument from design
this argument stands on the acceptance of Leibniz's windowless monads, saying that if all clocks keep time without any causal interaction, there must be some outside force that regulated them. but the issue comes from how the monadology states the monads cannot ever interact, so thus cannot know there are any others. to it the whole mirroring of the universe may even be a dream, Leibniz contends it is, but all their dreams are synchronised. this is fantastical but compared to Cartesianism, is practically credible. but Leibniz extends this argument into what is called the argument from design. Philonous contends that the brain being sensible and regarding senses, must exist only to the mind.

##### C. KANT'S THEORY OF SPACE AND TIME
Kant sets out his theory of space of time in his *Critique of Pure Reason*, saying perception is due partly to what is perceived and what does the perceiving, as explained in [[Books/Completed/The History of Philosophy|The History of Philosophy]]. regarding space and time, he more fleshes out arguments of space than time, as he argues that they are essentially similar. of his metaphysical arguments of space, they are as follows:

1. space is not empirical, for space is presupposed by referring to sensations external, and thus external experience is only possible through presentation of space.
2. space is a necessary presentation a priori, which underlies all external perception, we cannot imagine a world with no space, but we can imagine that there should be nothing in space.
3. space is not a genal concept of the relations of things, as there is only 1 space, which what we call "spaces" are only sections.
4. space is presented in infinite magnitude, which holds within itself all parts of space.. this is different to the concept instance relation, so thus space is not conceptual but an intuition.

the 1st arguments a place which exists outside the current position of perception. in a way you would be placed in the noumenal world, as experience is akin to a cloak-room attendant giving different coats to different people. but what substantiates why perception is as it is? for instance why do eyes always exist above the mouth. Kant deduces that the mind arranges the external world of space, but never finds it necessary to justify why it does order it in the specific way it does. this also applies to causality, meaning causality is assumed but cannot be justified.

the 2nd argument seems unserious for being based on the pretence of what can and cannot be imagined, but is empirically wrong to suppose we can imagine a space with nothing in it. any concept of empty space is couched in ideas of what exists in our perceived space, and thus we are not imagining space with nothing in it but the *perception* of a space with nothing in it. even outer space which is physically empty we constitute as a "vacuum", which is an invented concept.

the 3rd argument can be summarised as denying the plurality of space. this argument falls a part as with the modern concepts of space and spaces, the argument becomes impossible to be stated and thus the argument falls a part.

the 4th argument is primary concerned with proving space to be intuition and not conceptual. it seems preposterous to even suppose that space cannot be conceptually devised but only sensed, and thus this argument is also rejected.

#### 28 Bergson
Bergson was one of the leading French philosophers of the 20th century, influencing William James and Whitehead, also largely influencing French thought, mainly in a conservative vein culminating in Vichy France. But his appeal was no laid directly in politics, being a general philosophical revolt against reason which began with Rousseau. it is difficult to classify Bergson's philosophy since it does not lay in any classification of empirical or a priori or realist or idealist, cutting across them all. another more apt way to classify philosophies more appropriate for Bergson's is along the lines of what desire drove the philosopher, be it happiness (optimism or pessimism), a love of knowledge (the great systems of philosophy), of love of action (pragmatists and Bergson (*and my own note of the Marxists*)).

to Bergson, the world is dualistic, separated into two disparate portions, one life and the other matter, or what the intellect views as inert. the universe is the clash of the two, life climbing upwards and matter falling downwards. life has struggled against matter since the beginning of time, wishing to break away and gradually learning to use matter for it's own ends despite still struggling against it. always seeking new outlets and greater liberty against the walls of matter. Evolution in this context has no end to the goal of adaption, but a continual travelling along the road approaching such adaption but never reaching it. Bergson views wants as inherent but a solution to that want unknown until it is realized, pushing against determinism in favor of free will. he views time and space as separate, space being the snapshot of becoming in matter and time the essential characteristic of mind. he says the mathematical time we think of is but an aspect of space, the time he speaks of being duration. Bergson explains duration to be the form consciousness refrains from separating past from present, combining them into one whole without distinction. he says that questions of relation of subject and object being to the frame of time rather than space, seeing the relation as the culmination of the past and present collapsing together and thrust forward to materialize it but never truely reach an end, as all is becoming.
