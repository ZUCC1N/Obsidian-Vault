# Preface
"the aim of the book is to draw a limit to thought, or rather—not to thought, but to the expression of thoughts: for in order to be able to draw a limit to thought, we should have to find both sides of the limit thinkable (i.e. we should have to be able to think what cannot be thought).""

"If this work has any value, it consists in two things: the first is that thoughts are expressed in it, and on this score the better the thoughts are expressed... the greater will be its value... May others come and do it better... the second thing in which the value of this work consists is that it shows how little is achieved when these problems are solved."

# 1 The world is all that is the case.
self explanatory, but the ""world"" is all that there could be, nothing lies outside. an object is an idea not a thing. the world is defined by the individual but also totality of ideas. in a circular manner draws back that if the world is ideas, ideas inform the world and the world is all there is, then the totality of ideas informs what truth there is and is not.
the world is informed by facts but also facts are informed by dividing the world. either things are true, or not true, without effecting anything else in the total truth.
# 2 What is the case... is the existence of states of affairs.
for a thing to be a thing, it needs to be part of a state. a object is inseparable from the states it could belong to, the truths it informs, and thus the truths also inform the object. an object is simple, anything more complex is a collection of simple objects. this means everything is objective, if the collection of object together is true then its complex must also be, and that is the only way for truth to exist. despite ideas being immaterial, they need to have some sort of connection to the material. a picture is a representation of an object, it is not the object itself. it does not imbue the object with substance, but simply captures an aspect of it, spacial, colour etc. from this, its easy to see a picture cannot be determined to be the truth or false without comparing it to what it represents, the picture alone cannot indicate if it is true or not.
# 3 A logical picture of facts is a thought.
Ludwig posits that all thoughts are logical as the world is borne of logic, we do not know illogic so cannot have thoughts on it. he moves on to the idea that language is the picture of an object, meaning language has the same issues as pictures that it cannot capture the innate substance of an idea but only its form.
# 4 A thought is a proposition with a sense.
Lud preposes that most questions are nonsensical so are not worthy of discussion, only purely logical propositions with sense are valid. of these logical thoughts, they like notation and text and pictures relate to the logic but hold the sense but not form. propositions alone do not dictate if they are true, but **do** depict reality **if** they are true. he also posits that thoughts should not be reliant on information or objects that do not directly pertain to them (you shouldn't need to know what black or white or paper is to be able to say a black point on a piece of paper is a point, you should simply be able to articulate it to be a point). from this, double negation without this sense results in situations where you do not get $p$ back form $\sim\sim p$, breaking the idea of what p is.

he states that the totality of propositions is natural science, but philosophy is not a natural science, being more the task of navigating and defining the boundaries of propositions. it defines what is relevant thoughts, and irrelevant thoughts.
