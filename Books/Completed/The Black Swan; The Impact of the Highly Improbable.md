### Prologue
#### On the Plumage of Birds
Taleb discusses the Black swan, a exception to the rule of all swans being white that was discovered with Australia. this shows how as humans we are prone to assume things are true until a single counterexample comes, in which we then question our predictions. the issue comes with how we fail to know what we have assumed, making predicting changes in the world and our views hard.
##### What you Do Not Know
Taleb provides an example of 9/11 to show this idea, were if we in general knew it was coming on the 10th we would have had precautions to make sure it wouldn't have happened. something might have happened, we don't know, but 9/11 wouldn't have. but also yet there were things known that should have stopped it, yet it didn't.
##### Experts and "Empty Suits"
Taleb states that he finds it quite laughable that experts claim to have 30 year forecasts but fail to even make correct predictions for a years time. he states the best way to take advantage of black swans is to purposefully expose yourself to them, as in most cases they offer little risk for an inconceivable reward.
##### Learning to Learn
Taleb explains that he doesn't think that events that happen really inform us of our general rules, but very specific ones (don't go up tall buildings cause they can fall down). he explains this as we don't really think, making sense for the amount of time humans have had the luxury of thinking and not instinctually running from predators relatively.
#### A New Kind of Ingratitude
Taleb expands this view of humans general ignorance with the idea there are many people that get recognition after death, but exponentially more that don't for the same or greater achievements had effects on the world. he provides the example of a legislator passing a law the day before 9/11 to stop it, but to the general population as 9/11 didn't happen, he has just wasted money, is kicked out and doesn't even feel himself that he did anything right. the recognition we feel he should have is only because of events he would have prevented, the hypotheitcal people wouldn't have the same view. "Who is more valuable, the politician who avoids a war or the one who starts a new one (and is lucky enough to win)?".
#### Life is Very Unusual
Taleb says the best way to study uncertainty is to study outliers, as you do not examine the best case scenario to inform the performance as the worst case may far out weigh it.
#### Plato and the Nerd
Taleb brings up the example of jewish gods being enraged when romans had statues of Caligula erected in temples in exchange for Yahweh to have statues in Roman ones, despite Yahweh having no material form (as is the point of a god to Abrahamic religions). he coins Platonicity, where we mistake material knowledge for an invalid assumption, we assume we know when we dont.
#### Too Dull to Write About
Taleb apologises for not covering certain topics, but also cope cause he thinks they are boring. he insinuates he wont get into [[Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus|Wittgenstein]] type problems as he sees overly wordy and deep quandaries don't have pertinence to anyone but the people that ask them, and he wants to discuss a thing that effects the average person; the philosophy of randomness and how to cope with it.
#### The Bottom Line
in conclusion to his introduction, he outlines the book will heavily focus on the uncertainty we live in, despite how we tend to ignore it, and because of this ignorance we will struggle to make accurate predictions in the coming future if we do not amend it.
