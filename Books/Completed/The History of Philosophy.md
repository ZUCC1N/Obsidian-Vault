## Part I Ancient Philosophy
### Philosophy before Plato
There is a barrier to understanding antiquity properly: the fall of the Roman Empire. in 313 CE Emperor Constantine gave Christianity legal status and protection by the Edict of Milan, and in 380 CE Emperor Theodosius I decreed by the Edict of Thessalonica that Christianity was to be the official religion of the Empire, outlawing others. From the fourth century CE much existing art of antiquity was lost, much on purpose. Christian zealots smashed statues and temples, defaced paintings and burned ‘pagan’ books. It has been estimated that as much as 90 per cent of the literature of antiquity was destroyed. The Christians took the stones of temples to build their churches, and over-wrote the manuscripts of the philosophers and poets with their scripture.  this combined with the fact at the time Christianity existed in a number of competing schools caused infighting in the religion aswell. the Roman Christians were also assisted by barbarians who held no value to the texts. fewer books were written and published, prohibitions on what could be read, leading to increased ignorance and narrowmindedness. only those texts regarded as most significant and outstanding managed to survive (and they were not entirely regarding everyone they should have).

what we know of [[Plato]]’s predecessors has come to us in parts. There are two kinds of sources for our knowledge of them: fragments, which are quotations from them, and testimonia, which are reports, paraphrases or summaries, both only given by later writers. Plato and [[Aristotle]] both summarized and quoted Presocratic thinkers, even if sometimes inaccurately. The next notable source is [[Cicero]], writing in the first century BCE, Thus a lack of reliability was already becoming apparent. The second century CE saw the sceptic philosopher [[Sextus Empiricus]] quote heavily from the Presocratics on knowledge and perception, while [[Plutarch]]’s *Moralia* quotes them on a wider range of topics. [[Alexander of Aphrodisias]] quoted a number of Presocratics in his commentary on Aristotle. One of the most useful sources for the history of Greek philosophy is *The Lives of the Philosophers* by [[Diogenes Laertius]], written in the third century CE. this hopefully demonstrates how threadbare our connection back can be, relying heavily on second hand sources of second hand sources of second hand sources.

#### Anaximander
[[Anaximander]] was a student of Thales reportedly, but was much more out there than him. He is credited with being the first person to draw a map of the known world and predicted an earthquake. Anaximander was said by Eusebius to have developed gnomons for identifying ‘solstices, timespans, hours horai and equinoxes’. Anaximander thought that humans came originally from fish, and so abstained from eating them on grounds of being related to them. He said that the sun is pure fire and larger than the earth, moonlight being a reflection of the sun's light. he also stated rain was caused by precipitation of water vapor, he stated the earth was a cylinder (but also Diogenes Laertius disagrees saying he said it was a sphere) and floats in the infinite, having no reason to move.

but like Thales, Anaximander’s most distinctive thesis concerns the arche. He said that the apeiron, ‘the infinite’ or ‘indefinite’, is that from which everything comes into being and returns to, as it is destined to keep balance. A surviving quote of Anaximander reads: 
<p style="text-align: center;"><i>
‘where things have their origin there too their passing away occurs according to necessity; they pay justice and reparation to one another for their injustice in conformity with the ordinance of time.’ <br>
Anaximander (610-546 BCE)
</i></p>
he believed the world existed in an equilibrium between the elements, and would naturally balance them in order to keep unity. [[Plutarch]] says in reference to Anaximander:
<p style="text-align: center;"><i>
‘the infinite is the universal cause of the generation and destruction of the universe. From it the heavens were separated off and in general all the worlds, infinite in number. He asserted that destruction, and, much earlier, generation occur from time immemorial, all the same things being renewed.’
 <br>
Plutarch (610-546 BCE)
</i></p>
[[Aristotle]] suggests in *Physics* that Anaximander held this belief as the infinite can have no other purpose than to be a principle, and can itself have no principle – that is, cannot derive from anything more fundamental than itself, for if it did it would not be a principle: 
<p style="text-align: center;"><i>
‘the region outside the heavens is infinite, then body and worlds also seem to be infinite, for why should there be “here” rather than “there” in the void? If body is anywhere, then it is everywhere. Again, if void and space are infinite, body too must be infinite – for with eternal things there is no difference between being possible and being actual... generation and destruction will come to an end unless there is something infinite from which what comes into being is subtracted.’<br>
Aristotle (384-322 BCE)
</i></p>
#### Anaximenes
a younger colleague of Anaximander, [[Anaximenes]] learned from both him and Thales. He agreed with Anaximander that the arche is infinite, but agreed with Thales in thinking that it was material, being *aer*, or a sort of dense moist air.
<p style="text-align: center;"><i>
‘Anaximenes … like Anaximander declares that the underlying nature is one and unlimited but not indeterminate, as Anaximander held, but definite, saying that it is air. It differs in rarity and density according to the substances it becomes. Becoming finer, it turns to fire; when condensed, it comes to be wind, then cloud; and when further condensed, it becomes water, then earth, then stones, and the rest come to be from these. He makes motion eternal, and says that change also comes to be through the eternal motion of the air.’ 
 <br>
Theophrastus (371-287 BCE)
</i></p>
Anaximenes held that the earth is flat and sits on top of air. Earthquakes occur when the earth is either too dry or too wet, for when too dry it cracks, when too wet it falls apart. The sun, moon and stars are flat fires but are too distant for us to feel their heat. The sun does not circle underneath the eart but instead rides round the circumference of earth, night is simply the time where the sun is on the other side of the earth. Anaximenes is notable as his arche accounts for *how* it constitutes different substances, unlike Thales, being heat and density:
<p style="text-align: center;"><i>
‘As Anaximenes of old believed, let us leave neither the cold nor the hot in the category of substance, but as common attributes of matter, which come as the results of its changes. For he declares that the contracted state of matter and the condensed state is cold, whereas what is fine and ‘loose’ (calling it this way with this very word) is hot. As a result he claimed that it is not said unreasonably that a person releases both hot and cold from his mouth. For the breath becomes cold when compressed and condensed by the lips, and when the mouth is relaxed, the escaping breath becomes warm because of rareness.’ 
 <br>
Plutarch (610-546 BCE)
</i></p>

A major achievement of the Pythagorean school was its discovery that the pitch of a musical note depends on the length of a string, and that simple numerical ratios explain the consonant intervals of the scale: 2:1 octave, 3:2 perfect fifth, 4:3 perfect fourth, and so on. If different lengths of each are plucked, they sometimes sound dissonant and sometimes consonant. This latter observation underlies measurement of consonant intervals – an interval being the distance between two notes, and a consonant interval being one in which the two notes sound good together. Experiment will show that if you have two lengths of string where one is half the length of the other will yield a consonance – this is the octave. If the second string is plucked at two-fifths the length of the first, the resulting consonant is a perfect fifth.

This discovery has been hailed as the first step of true science, because it provides a quantified measure of a physical phenomena. The Pythagoreans thought of the heavenly bodies as emitting a hum as they flew through space, the distances between them being such as to form a scale: earth and moon are a tone apart, moon to Mercury a semi-tone, Venus to the sun a minor third; Mars to Jupiter and Jupiter to Saturn are each a semi-tone, and Saturn to the sphere of the fixed stars a minor third. For the Pythagoreans as for others – including Plato – the idea of harmony came to have more than just the mathematical significance of ratios producing consonances; it became a key metaphor in thinking about matters ethical and psychological also.

Pythagoras is credited with thinking that the earth is a sphere, and later writers also claimed that he thought the cosmos is heliocentric, which is why the Copernican heliocentric model of the universe was described as ‘Pythagorean’. #### Xenophanes
Xenophanes’ returns to the school of thought of Thales, Anaximander and Anaximenes in his rejection of divination, believing that natural phenomena such as earthquakes and rainbows are not messages from the gods, but should be investigated and understood. he took an interest in the natural world, noting the presence of fossils of fish and seaweed on mountain tops, speculating about meteorological phenomena and the boundaries of our world. he thought that the earth extended indefinitely downwards, and that means the sun could not circle beneath it at night. Instead there is a new sun every day, gathered together out of ‘many small fires’.

Xenophanes anecdotally was not a fan of Pythagoras, reportedly claiming to hear a dead friend’s voice in a dog’s howls. He was scathing about Homer and Hesiod because they made the gods seemingly human. He said that ‘if oxen and horses or lions had hands, and could paint with their hands, and produce works of art as men do, horses would paint the forms of the gods like horses, and oxen like oxen.’ He seems to have thought that the earth is being dissolved into the sea: ‘All human beings are destroyed when the earth has been carried down into the sea and turned to mud. This change takes place for all the worlds.’

But, whatever Xenophanes actually meant, there are suggestive hints in these views that relate him to Parmenides. Aristotle says in the Metaphysics that Xenophanes was the first to argue that reality is ‘One’, and Plato called Xenophanes ‘the first of the Eleatics’, these being the philosophers of Parmenides’ school who subscribed to the doctrine that reality is a single unchanging eternal thing. Aristotle went further to suggest that Xenophanes thought that the world and god are the same thing; in one fragment indeed he says that the world and god are ‘equal every way’.

#### The Sophists
In the original meaning of the word, a ‘sophist’ was an educated person with expertise in one or more fields of learning, akin to a polymath. By the 5th century BCE it had evolved to designate someone who's profession was the service of teaching the techniques of rhetoric and the background knowledge in literature and thought needed to employ it correctly. Greek was still an oral society, so sophists made a good living in this line of work. Socrates and Plato disliked the sophists for being paid a salary for teaching, and for educating on the art of persuasion and how to win arguments, not how to discover truth.

#### Cynicism
the Cynics in a literal sense wished to live according to nature free of conventions. their founder, Antisthenes, first studied under Gorgias but then become a pupil before becoming a follower of Socrates upon hearing his theories of virtue relating to asceticism. he thought this meant virtue could be taught through self control and courage. it is expressed through actions and should act according to virtue and not the laws of the state. Antisthenes was also a lower born Athenian and due to being a bastard with a Thracian mother lacked Athenian citizenship. he had many spats with Plato for his aristocratic tendencies. he embraced the life of an ascetic beggar, only having a worn down cloak, a staff and a thing to carry random things. he also invented the cynic concept you only need a folded cloak to sleep.

Diogenes the Cynic pushed the concept of living according to nature to it's limit. he began his life banished from Sinope on the crime of him and his father debasing the coinage. when arriving in Athens he followed Antisthenes around like a dog, causing one of the potential sources of the name "cynic" or perhaps the doggish lifestyle Diogenes took on. in Greek dog is kyon and dog-like kynikos. Diogenes believed you should live simply and naturally like a hound, he would relive himself in public and steal foods from temples that were for offering. he desired to live completely independent with convention, saying he was a citizen of the world or a cosmopolitan. he desired only happiness and clarity of mind though asceticism and self-sufficiency. he had no shame for simply living a simple virtuous life.

Finally came Crates of Thebes, who began life with great wealth but gave it away upon hearing of Diogenes. he also wished to be a cosmopolitan, so he and his wife Hipparchia of Maroneia, another wealthy born individual decided to live as beggars in Athens. they became known for their good humour and principled stances and were welcomed everywhere, mostly as mediators for family disputes. they were less confrontational than Diogenes and thus acheived something closer to ataraxia.

#### Scepticism
the Sceptics were characterised by their questioning of if knowledge was even possible, being active from the 3rd century BCE to the 2nd century CE. many Presocratics purported to doubt the senses, and Sophists were capable of arguing effectively for either side of an argument. so what really is the truth? Socrates reportedly took a greater interests in ethics due to this seemingly fruitless endeavour when how one should live remained unanswered. Plato also seemingly had a undercurrent of this doubt, in his dialogues he presents the idea knowledge of the world is only probabilistic, and can be easily relative between people. this is the solution scepticism offers, saying that this all indicates there is in fact no knowledge to be found. sceptics manifested in 2 forms, the Platonic Academy, and the Pyrrhonian School.

it was the 6th successor to Plato, Arcesilaus who changed the direction of the academy in 266 BCE. his method embraced the dialectical method to highlight the indecisiveness that comes about. he said you should withhold belief in general, as no side meets the criterion of truth, as no criterion exists. this issue with this as to the ancient mind such as Aristotle, the Stoics and Epicureans belief and perceptions were the same thing, and actions are based on perceptions. if you act with no beliefs you are simply acting in mindlessness, or you cannot act at all. Arcesilaus responds to this that action is also withheld by reason in the same way he argues you should withhold belief. if you acted in response to all perceptions you would act in contradictory ways or even be paralysed. thus action only occurs "in conformity with the reasonable". it is not what is the most reasonable or truthful course of action but simply what seems the most optimal from experience. Cicero said "he knew nothing, not even his own ignorance." this implies he does think truth exists but we simply are unable of obtaining it.

his successor Carneades famously argued in Rome in 155 BCE in favour of justice and then the next day against it in a sophistic style, highlighting the belief in the impossibility of knowledge. unlike Arcesilaus he slightly soften his sceptical stance allowing for some role of belief. he maintained there is no criterion of truth in sense-perception, but there is a more modest criterion that we can sue to help us choose the actions of a practical life, "the persuasive". this was not persuasive in that it was causally persuasive, but simply like "plausibility" or some sort of rational persuasion. you act on what is the most persuasive impression you are given regardless of it's truth. for really important matters they would act in the unifying impression, the one that has no competing impressions to it. happiness comes from the persuasive, unified and thoroughly tread, as they have been examined in their entirety. after his successor's successor, Philo of Larissa, the academy reverted to a more orthodox platonic doctrine, 

the other school, Pyrrhonism unlike trying to distinguish belief and truth was more focused on obtaining *ataraxia*, or a kind of peace of mind. it's name sake, Pyrrho began life as a painter, but upon reading Democritus he turned to philosophy. he soldiered with Alexander as far as India, picking up a sort of "most noble philosophy" from Gymnosophists, focusing on agnostic and suspension of judgement. nothing is just or unjust, honourable or dishonourable, and in general nothing exists, reality is an illusion. thus, we should have no views of anything as it's inconsequential, and refuse to take sides on anything. reportedly this realisation would begin first with speechlessness, but eventually a freedom from worry.

Pyrrhonian scepticism gets it's major statement from a later figure, Aenesidemus. he argued neither sense-experience or thought can create knowledge, for even in thought you can argue for one side with opposing evidence contradicting it allowing for you to argue for the other side too. Aenesidemus set out 10 modes or tropes to which are the grounds for why we should suspend judgement. it is whittled down to that perception is subjective, so there is no definitive perception, and thus no definitive experience of judgement. with this there can be no definitiveness on what anything is. another source later one identified as Agrippa summarised it into five tropes, as reported by Sextus Empiricus:

<p style="text-align: center;"><i>
Any subject of debate prompts conflicting views among both ordinary people and philosophers, making it impossible to accept or reject either side of the dispute. So we must suspend judgment.
<br>
When one tries to justify a claim in an argument, one has to appeal to a prior claim. But this in turn must be justified – and so on in a regress that never ends. So we must suspend judgment.
<br>
Everything is relative: things appear as they do only depending on the conditions or circumstances of their being perceived or judged. So we must suspend judgment.

All attempts at judgment rest on assumptions, but alternative assumptions could be invoked, making it impossible to decide. So we must suspend judgment.

We often enough discover that in trying to confirm a judgment we find ourselves already invoking considerations implicit in that judgment; this is circular. So we must suspend judgment.

#### Neoplatonism
Neoplatonism acts as an extension of Plato's doctrine, adding a comprehensive view of the universe and our  relationship with it. it was an alternative religion to Christianity but also an inspiration for it. it rejects the materialism of the Epicureans and Stoics, or more specifically that reality is only physical and instead argued mind is the ultimate basis of reality. the label of Neoplatonism is misleading but, the followers claimed to be Platonists as they valued Plato the most of their predecessors, but they aswell as reviving his views used it as a basis foundation to combine nearly all preceding debates besides the materialist views.

the two base principles of Neoplatonism is that mind is fundamental opposed to matter, and that the ultimate cause of everything is a single unitary principle. they thought this principle was more "real" than reality. so thus mind is related to this unitary principle, and thus intrinsically linked to reality. the founder of this doctrine was Plotinus, studying under Ammonius of Alexandria a Platonist that combined aspects of Plato and Aristotle. his doctrine built of the criticism of Plato that had amassed over the 6 centuries since he lived. he accepted the dubiously attributed *letters* to be genuine and seemed to know the speculated unwritten teachings of Aristotle. middle Platonism consisted of the Academy's doctrine after the sceptic phase of the Academy, combining elements of Aristotle and Stoics with Plato. Plotinus broke off from this doctrine to create his own doctrine. he drew form it and mystical elements of Orphism and Judaism, making it palatable for audiences that aligned with them. later instances included elements of theurgy.

#### Boethius
Anicius Manlius Severinus Boethius was a Roman Aristocrat born around the fall of the Western Roman Empire. he lived in the time of the Ostrogothic emperor Theodoric, who due to be educated in Constantinople allowed for the continuation of the roman empires culture as if they had never been conquered. Boethius knew Greek and Latin, and may have continued studying he had not taken up office in Ravenna. this was a mistake due to the danger associated, leading to him being arrested and tried for treason due to court intrigue, he was presumably executed but while in captivity wrote his *Consolation of Philosophy*. in his youth he seeked to translate and commentate all the works of Plato and Aristotle, also writing on Porphyry and Cicero. he also wrote textbooks on arithmetic and logic, which was his chief passion. his works on Porphyry covers a set of questions around universals around properties or qualities but are set aside due to being too difficult for an introductory work. however, he does ask the question do universals exist separate to what they apply to, or are they only particulars that are concepts we use to describe similarities between things. if they do exist separately, are they physical or not. and how do they interact with their particulars. Boethius argues that they exist not separately but the concepts themselves are not empty, as to him if universals are not independent, talk of them would constitute nothing. his view is they exist but always and only in the particulars they apply to. their thought is universal but in existence particular. this is inline with the nominalists in medieval philosophy, for which the problem of universals was an important topic.

His theory of free will in light of god also aligns with Augustine. if god knows the future the future must be fixed, thus free will cannot exist. but if this follows then humans are set in stone on their actions and thus cannot be blamed or praised for their actions. Boethius' solution is god is external to time and thus time is ever-present to him. but still it's argued that this does not negate the argument as if god sees that future humans are still beholden to the future god sees. Boethius says this inevitability is only relative to god, not the future of mortals. this distinction between the divine and mortals is left unclear as it's specifics. the choices of god's foreknowledge is to deny it exists, or deny free will, neither is satisfactory to theologists regarding sin and divinity. this can be circumvented by taking a Greek view of fate, ala Oedipus Rex where he knows his fate but is beholden to it no matter how hard he fights it.

*Consolation of Philosophy* has great philosophical merit, taking the form of a dialogue, a mixture of prose and verse between Boethius imprisoned and Philosophy in the form of a woman. he despairs at his poor fortune, but she offers him consolation, not of sympathy but rather reminds him happiness lies elsewhere than human luck. he complains how he is virtuous but suffers while the wicked prosper, but Philosophy says he will disprove him. she distinguishes between worldly goods and riches and true goods like virtue and love. the latter he has not lost, so despite losing his status he did not lose real happiness. she argues goodness and happiness are the same thing, so the wicked are implicitly unhappy. everything occurs due to god's providence so things will eventually turn and suffering now may have a point in the grand scheme. Boethius thinks this proves there is no freewill, but Philosophy responds with the above doctrine of god being separated from time. commentators imply this work goes against his Christian faith, some saying that it is in fact satire of pagan philosophy to display how it is insufficient, but this is a stretch as his views here seem consistent with his other works. however medieval thought did not think him being ironic, he became a standard source of study for logic. his Consolation became his most read work, it was admired and translated up to the 18th century.

but Anselm is best known for his Ontological Argument, saying that god is the greatest concept conceived, but if it existed only within the mind, you could conceive something greater than it that does actually exist. thus the greatest thing that exists, must exist. Kant criticises it for making existing a property that exists on par with any other property, not a condition of being able to have such properties. if you suppose there is a certain table, you can paint it, chop length of the legs and it still exists, but if you could changes it's existence to non-existence, can you leave the colour and height the same? that's nonsensical a nonsensical question. the issue is that it tries to treat atheism as a case of contradiction by saying existence is an essential part of his nature. but he relies entirely on the concept that god has all positive attributes. he conflates mental attributes and physical attributes without discerning they have a keen difference. and what does saying something "exists" is a greater thing, does this mean better. does this making anything that is real automatically better than thought? is killing someone more moral than thinking of killing someone. the most effective argument against Anselm is provided by his contemporary Benedictine monk Gaunilo Marmoutiers with his "Lost Island" argument. he suggests you to think of the greatest island you can think of. this is a meaningful statement and you can certainly conceive such an island. but following Anselm's argument, the greatest island has to exist in reality to be the greatest island. therefore, an objectively single greatest island exists, which is obviously nonsensical. there still are philosophers that try to devise an argument like Anselm's, but interestingly, the status of god's existence is almost never the reason someone chooses their beliefs.

#### Aquinas
Thomas Aquinas was born into an aristocratic family in the Lazio region of Italy, being educated at the Benedictine abbey and the University of Naples. while in Naples he was recruited by the recently formed Dominican. his family tried to dissuade him due to feeling them to be hippies, going to the extent to imprison him and attempt to seduce him from his zealotry. when he was allowed to leave he went to Paris to study at university under Albertus Magnus. at Cologne he wrote commentaries on the Old Testament and Peter Lombard's Sentences, Boethius and a collection of quodlibetical responses to theological questions. this lead him to one of his most famous works, the *Summa Contra Gentiles*. later on Aquinas was sent back to Paris by his Dominican superiors to combat a version of Aristotelianism associated with Averroes, which proposed a more sanitised version void of Neoplatonic elements added over the course of time. due to Aquinas being an Aristotelian who opposed this interpretation, under took combating it. a result of this was some elements of his doctrine was condemned by the Bishop of Paris, causing his reputation to suffer. it wasn't until his canonisation and placement next to the Doctors of the Church that his reputation began to recover. in 1879 CE Pope Leo XIII declared his teachings the definitive catholic doctrine. a specific issue his doctrine covers is the nature of the soul. he describes material substance as a compound of essence and a set of accidents. the essence is itself the matter and form that constitutes the substance, while accidentals are all the things it perhaps could have but also perhaps could not. to Aquinas the human essence is the soul. the body can have an accident of obesity of anorexia but the form of a human remains due to the soul. but his view isn't entirely devoid of Platonic aspects, he says the soul subsists after the death of a body and is incorruptible. but it is not a substance itself, as substances are "complete". #### William of Ockham

he saw himself as an innovator, leaving behind the old ways of discussing philosophy, logic and theology in order to pursue a new way with nominalism at it's centre.  Occam's Razor – an alternative spelling of his last name – saying there is no need to complicate things when a simple solution is sufficient. this doctrine is illustrated by his theories of knowledge and metaphysics. opposed to Aquinas he argued theological truth is only given by faith, not reason. saying god creating things independent of concepts of logic and rationality. thus he argues there is no proof of god's existence that can suffice. he also thought god could have incarnated as a human or an animal, or even a human and an animal at the same time, which received criticism from his peers. he argued that god is the only necessary thing that must exist, everything else must be discovered through enquiry. he was a nominalist, thinking there are no separately existing universals or essences. his theory of universals might be considered a version of conceptualism, that universals are conceptual and just the mental groups we give to things with similarities. he agreed that contingent knowledge comes from sense, but added that unlike animals human minds give us the power to go beyond simple perception and create knowledge through memories and abstractions. like many other empirical nominalists he was interested in Aristotle's physics and argued that some of Aristotle's categories are unnecessary. he was advanced in logic, discovering a form of De Morgan's Theorems and investigating 3 valued logic.

because of the quarrel between Emperor and pope, Ockham advocated for a secular government. he argued the emperor and pope were of equal value and power but in different spheres of responsibility, quoting the 2nd epistle of Timothy that the church should own no lands or property. in essence the church was purely spiritual in power. he was a voluntarist on free will, thinking the will to be free of mind and natural appetites to the extent it can stray from what appears good. this is opposed to Aristotle and Aquinas who both argued the will is subordinate to reason. later contemporaries of Ockham such as Jean Buridan attempted to reconcile these views to say the will can suspend itself, or defer the making of a choice when faced with undecidable alternatives. this lead to 'Buridan's Ass', a reduction of his view where a donkey starves due to being unable to choose between 2 equally distanced bales of hay. the bitterness of Ockham's ideas combined with his excommunication lead to the expelling of Ockhamists from the University of Paris, but themselves accused Thomists and Scotists of heresy themselves. the debate between the old school and new schools of philosophy extended beyond the realism-nominalism debate but how Aristotle is to be interpreted entirely.
### Philosophy in the Renaissance
the art of the medieval times was dominated by images of annunciations, flagellations, crucifixions, depositions and resurrections. the Renaissance sees a far wider palette of subjects, landscapes, still lives, picnics, festivals, portraits, nudes, battles and legendary mythological scenes. there was a turn from the temptations and tears to a celebration of life itself and a refreshment of poets, thinkers and artists. it was a significant turn to a more generalised study of philosophy, not just the technicalities of scripture. the modern rise of continental thoughts with the work of Jacques Derrida and Michel Foucault may seem to be a similar phenomena. logic, ethics and rhetoric began to be studied more widely again. Plato and Aristotle began to be studied in their original forms, not Arab translations or neo-platonic readings. but this also saw a rise in the study of magic, alchemy, astrology and the Hermetic and Kabbalistic studies. the occult sciences played to the desires of eternal youth, immortality and wealth.
#### Renaissance Platonism
the Platonist revival began in 1439 CE, where Plethon, the leading scholar in Byzantium of the time and Neoplatonist gave a lecture in Florence on Plato and how he differs from Aristotle, to Plato's benefit and Aristotle's detriment. they were later published under the title *De Differentiis Aristotelis et Platonis*. this naturally sparked controversy, people attempting to defend Aristotle and claiming Platonism to be a religion that wished to rival Christianity. but Plethon had achieved what he desired sparking interest in Plato. the fall of Constantinople in 1453 CE drove scholars such as John Argyropoulos out, some settling in Italy. Marsilio Ficino was Argyropoulos pupil and translated Plato's work into Latin and headed a new Platonic Academy in Florence. he also translated Plotinus, Porphyry, Iamblichus, and less helpfully, the Hermetic Corpus. the Platonic Academy was more a club than a place of study, but it was a single influential body for preaching Platonic ideas in 15th century Europe. Ficino also wrote a work entitled *Platonic Theology* which seeked to reconcile Plato with Christianity. he said souls with their immortality are the link between word of ideas and material world.  he coined the term 'Platonic love' to describe the soul's love for god when enlightenment gives us unmediated contact with him.
#### Renaissance Political Thought
the Italian city states during the renaissance began to independently evolve, becoming independent communes with their own systems of governance. with this a literature of advice on such topics flourished, outlining the aims and art of governance. technically they were subjects to the Holy Roman Empire and by the 13th century the study of roman law in universities had reached a level where the argument for the Emperor's sovereignty was solid. the paradox was the city states acted of their own accords, as if they were each their own republic. many leaders became dukes and princes instead of the political titles they would usually hold. most titles became hereditary, but some like the duke of Venice remained democratic, although only chosen from the aristocracy. but even then leaders would rarely flaunt their power up to the 15th century. there was also a doctrine premised on Saint Paul that rulers are appointed by god, and are in effect his agents of earth so should be respect as such. Italian city states did not completely gel with this idea, and upon the rediscovery of Aristotle's political philosophy and translation into Latin, city states were given academic basis for their dominion. this in effect gave birth to the basis of political science as a discipline, beginning with Aquinas to Machiavelli and beyond. Marsilius of Padua argued the political power should lay with the people, and the papacy and empire should be separated. his ultimate argument is the government exists to serve the people so should obey their will. but growing factionalism in city states showed a misplacement of his faith in democracy, as city after city went from quasi-democracy to oligarchy. the basis of this development had basis in governance's chief aim is to maintain order, so that the people can flourish, and with the added doctrine of Humanism should even pursue fame, not through military but through art.

Aquinas had maintained that peace often was maintained through force, being his 'just war' theory inherited by Augustine. early renaissance humanists disavowed such an idea, considering war barbaric for civil society. the only required thing is virtue like justice, generosity and freedom of sin, as such a ruler would never stir dissatisfaction in his people. but at the same time the literature began to slant to appease and gain favour with the rulers as their power increased, in effect mirroring what they would wish to hear. of specific note is Machiavelli and the Medicis, who ruled Florence and were the subject of *The Prince*. Machiavelli was in effect Florence's minister of foreign affairs and war, meaning he frequently travelled as emissary to Florence. this allowed him to view many different courts and study their systems to hopefully gain favour with them. but with the Medicis' return in 1512 CE his career in politics ended, and he was imprisoned and tortured on accusations of conspiracy. he was released and allowed to retire to his farm in the countryside of Florence, writing to express his anger at his deposition.

## Part III Modern Philosophy
### The Rise of Modern Thought
from the 4th to the 14th century CE, philosophy played second fiddle to theology, and as noted above it became increasingly dangerous to speculate philosophically on things that strayed from orthodox doctrine imposed by the church. the Reformation in the 16th century broke this grip, not due to the new intellectual liberalism but because the reformation stripped many Protestant clergy of power to enforce their orthodoxy on enquiry. this lead to a rise in the occult, but also the liberation of philosophy and science. of the scientific inquiry, Francis Bacon and Descartes lead the charge, and regarded thus as the founders of modern philosophy. they both rejected the Scholastics and the instructional Aristotelianism of the Church, but differed in their outlook. on one side, Bacon was an empiricist, on the other Descartes a rationalist in the epistemological sense of the term. this lead to the conventional group of 17th and 18th century philosophers into these 2 camps. Empiricists think all knowledge must be provable by tests or experience. rationalists believe true knowledge exists in rational inference from first principles of logic of are self evident. thus, empiricists flourish in the scientific study of nature, while rationalists mathematics and logic. this culminates in Immanuel Kant, who rejects both schools and proposes a synthesis of them. he thought both experience and thought shape the world, and neither is possible without the other.
#### Francis Bacon
Bacon believed in science being based on observation of facts, which was often caricatured as meaning to observe at random then draw a theory after the fact, which is not what he intended. this nonetheless was the commonly believed interpretation, even figures such as Darwin and Newton subscribing to it. what he truly intended was that observations shall be gathered to test a formulated hypothesis that specifically refutes or supports it. it in a way anticipates Mill's methods. like Socrates he believed this observation should be guided by experts of the topic, you should ask farmers of the efficiency of farming for example.

#### Hume
Hume is considered to be the culmination of what Locke and Berkley laid out, the end of the trio of important British empiricists, in fact some thinking at the time he had "finished off" empiricism regarding epistemology, by showing it collapses into scepticism, which in fact was not his intention. his most important work was his *Treatise of Human Nature* which he later regarded to be subpart, editing it unto *An Enquiry Concerning Human Understanding* and *An Enquiry Concerning the Principles of Morals*, but these edited and revised versions of his doctrine are considered by most to have lost some sense of granularity in his argument, so are usually disregarded in favour of the original Treatise. in Books I and II of the Treatise and the first Enquiry, Hume sets out the same problems that Locke and Berkley did, with some distinct differences. Locke wished a general theory of knowledge, being about relations of ideas with contingent or empirical knowledge only probabilistic. Berkley had offered a metaphysical answer to epistemology, the nature of reality explaining knowledge as the substance of reality is mind, and experience consists in having ideas. Hume saw that their views differed when they considered the nature of mind, so set himself to create a "philosophical psychology", which he called a theory of human nature. his aim went further than a explanation of knowledge and it's acquisition, Hume thinking such questions will illuminate important principles of ethics, which leads into Book III of the Treatise of the second Enquiry.

it's important to consider that Hume lived in a time that allowed for such debate of questions he raised, being The Enlightenment. before this period, ethics had been little discussed outside the Christian moral theology, and rarely any discussion of what underlies the principles of human Ethics. morality had to agree with scripture, and diverging was punished. but with the enlightenment the church lost this threat, so ethics could thus be more scrutinised and examined to determine what way is the best way of acting. a Christian would response "as god intends it", carrying a certain sense of reward of punishment for obeying or not, which creates an inherent power dynamic that wills you to obey. but in isolation, commands don't logically have a compelling reason to obey, making them unfit for the basis of morality. this is what prompted Hume, his enquiry being of morals come from a long chain of argument and induction of reason, or are induced immediately and innately. whether morals should be universal or subjective to a specific person. his general consensus is morals are innately decided. his primary basis for making a claim is his proposal that our operations of understanding and fundamental beliefs do not come about by reason but the way human nature is constituted, hence giving the name to the work itself.

Hume first describes his method of analysing human nature, excluding experience from consideration. he did this as he believed he was doing for morals what newton had done for science, stating principles of physics to define it. Hume sets forth the principle of the "association of ideas" to explain human nature, which in itself is able to explain experience, belief, causation, induction the concept of self and the limits of reason. in a vacuum, only considering consciousness and no aspects of the outside world, Hume notes that perception has 2 kinds: impressions and ideas. impressions and ideas differ in vividness, impressions being more clear than ideas. they can both be simple or complex, simple ideas being copies of simple impressions, and complex ideas built by comparing many simple impressions and ideas. we can imagine a city with streets of gold and walls or rubies due to knowing the parts, even without seeing them combined into that exact image. ideas are always impressions first, and thus no ideas are valid without an impression they were drawn from. Hume also emphasises that impressions are atomic, being the smallest unit and independent of each other. but ideas arise from combining impressions based on association. association can take the form of resemblance, ideas that are similar connect, contiguity in place or time meaning ideas that occur together, and cause and effect. Cause and effect is considered the most important. association also explains beliefs, to believe something an idea is more present in the mind with more "force or vivacity" and if one did not believe it or thinks of it neutrally. Hume says this commonly happens when an impression associated to an idea or another thing derived from an idea imparts some of it's force onto the idea increasing it's vividness. the more ideas become associated together the more our minds habitually run from one to the other. this is the basis of the basic human comprehension of cause and effect, we thinking causes "make" the effect occur, but if we truly analyse the "causal necessity" we come to find that there is no founding impression to link cause and perceived effect causally. we feel a desire to move from the cause to the effect and thus project this desire onto the world. this also explains our belief in a world independent of our experience, we are unable to not believe in them. philosophy cannot prove these things, as they transcend reason.

it is thus consistent then wen considering the self Hume says the self is nothing but a word we use to denote to be what we think is a single entity through time, citing that if one looks inside one's self to find an impression that informs the idea of the self, there can be none found besides a bundle of impressions and ideas that are constantly changing. we only think them to be the self due to the associations of them leading us to interpret them as such, in the same way as the external world and causality. Hume thought that once we understand the way minds function, just as we understand how physics function, we will all come to understand that the mind engages in 2 kinds of actions, comparing ideas, and inferring matters of fact. ideas are mathematic or logical propositions that can be related by intuitions, or by being demonstrated. matters of fact come from empirical observation or causal reasoning, and relate things about the world. this lead to a famous remark of Hume, that implies that if philosophy does not contribute to reasoning or empirical investigation of the world, it should be discarded, which went on to influence Positivists and the Analytic philosophers of the 20th century. 

it can be inferred that as Hume says morals are innate, and that he thinks topics such as self and causality due to being innate are unprovable, that there can be no philosophical proofs of morality. instead, we project our sentiments into the world in a subjective sense. this is the basis of subjectivism, or its 18th century variant, sentimentalism. it says that good and evil are only what we approve or disapprove of. born from the loosening of discussion of morality, there were 2 views born, the rationalist belief that morals are shown to us by reason, and that morality is born from the human feelings of benevolence and sympathy, being the view of sentimentalists. Hume thought reason was not enough motivation to action, emotion being the only thing that can do this. reason does not prompt but only guide once a prompt has already been given. Hume thought things such as murder are seen as immoral not due to any calculus but simply because humans have an innate disapproval of it, and thus morally distain it. this makes morals something that depends on your personal emotions of a topic, not a universal. a draw back of such a doctrine is it assumes that all people have a sound moral compass, but Hume believed this to be not necessary as he thought all people were fundamentally benevolent. he thought any variance came from lack of information, confusion, or only a poor moral sense (which i assume only means intensity of moral sense opposed to misaligned)
#### Rousseau
People who think the personal lives of thinkers are irrelevant to assessing their work have a valid source of support with Jean-Jacques Rousseau, who despite advancing theory of education that promotes sympathy for a child's view point in *Emile, or On Education* for instance, left all 5 children he fathered on the doorstep of the Founding Hospital in Paris the moment they were born. he promoted open human relationships but was generally paranoid of anyone who wished to help him. his principle fames lies partially in his *Discourse on Inequality*, which follows the lead of Locke by promoting a state of nature and arguing property to be the source of social and economic inequality. his natural man is interested in his own welfare, companionate and requires only "food, a woman and sleep" to be content. he is also known for his novel *Julie, Or the New Heloise*, presented a series of letters between the medieval Abelard and Heloise. it was a great bestseller, prompting a storm of emotion in readers, especially the profoundly poignant death of the heroine Julie. it gave the 18th century cult of sensibility a boost, and promoted tourism of the Alpine region.

in 1762 CE Rousseau published *The Social Contract* and *Emile, or On Education*. the first opens with his famous line:
<p style="text-align: center;"><i>
Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains.
</i></p>
the second in a section titled ***The Profession of a Savoyard Vicar*** sets out a unitarian defence of religion, contrary to the trinitarian theologies of Catholics and protestants. this would be enough to get him in trouble, but he also said all religious are equally of merit and there is no such thing as original sin or divine revelation. his theory of education argues that children's education should mirror the stages of human history, beginning in a natural state before slowly adding economic and social relations to life. his books marked a turn away from materialism and empiricism that marked the Enlightenment, causing him to fall out with Enlightenment thinkers. theologians banned his books, and exiled him for France and Switzerland. Voltaire and Frederick the Great offered to take him in. he instead went to England with Hume, gaining a celebrity status before opinion turned against him, and his paranoid inflamed after a prank by Horace Walpole, he fled England. he later studied Botany and wrote his *Confessions* the first autobiography of it's kind. he died of a stroke in the summer of 1778 CE, leaving his last work *Daydreams of a Solitary Walker* unfinished.

Rousseau is mainly known for his political philosophy, manly set out in *The Social Contract* as well as his *Discourse On the Origins of Inequality* and *On Political Economy*. at the end to *Discourse on Inequality* Rousseau expresses humanity emerging from the state of nature is unavoidable and fosters inequality and dependence on others as we become unable to fulfil our own needs. this threatens the stability of society, and thus, people are driven to established an authority to keep the peace. but this authority only institutionalises the inequality and dependency, giving it a force of law to be upheld, favouring the wealthy and powerful and exposing the lower classes to exploitation. Rousseau begins *The Social Contract* attempting to remedy this issue, bringing a key concept of "the general will". the specifics of "the general will" is unclear, but the two most common interpretations are it to be the will of the people in the democratic sense, or, more abstractly, a transcendental common purpose or interest that is shared between all people. both exist in the text, but the second interpretation more so. both imply to be an idealization that no state actually embody, which as a consequence if Rousseau is believed to have meant this, no state is politically legitimate. regardless of implication, "the general will" always is directed to the good of each and all people, reconciling the conflict of interest of state and individuals. Rousseau speaks of the demand to obey such a will as being "forced to be free". Kant is anticipated with Rousseau claiming moral freedom exists as people impose moral laws on themselves.

Rousseau disliked representative democracy and the Hobbesian idea of a sovereign elected by the people. he thought such arrangements involved an alienation of self rule, and thus a form of slavery. some believe Rousseau to say this as he wishes the sovereignty to lie in the people, the government being a tool to obey them. either way, he was pessimistic of politics. thinking government inevitably exercise power over it's people, citing history and the modern representative democracies of his time.
#### Kant
most accept that the 3 greatest figures of western philosophy are Plato, Aristotle and Kant. others are important, but these 3 stand above the rest in their impact, their intellectual power and penetration into the deepest sections of difficult questions and their scope of enquiry. each established new concepts and vocabulary to explore fundamental questions on metaphysics, epistemology and ethics, extending to psychology and social theory, enriching human thought. recall the conflict between empiricists and rationalists, concerning epistemology, the former arguing knowledge lies in perception and the latter in reason. inspired by Hume's notion of "the way our minds work" prompted him to create a theory of knowledge that reconciled the views of rationalists and empiricists. in essence his theory says sensory experience is organized and interpreted to make the world appear as it does by imposing rational a priori concepts onto them, the result being the world of experience. he calls the faculty of the mind that undertakes this "the understanding" which uses highly general concepts on sensory data to create experience. he uses "intuition" in it's modern context of "sense-experience" to denote incoming sensory data. the a priori concepts and incoming data are essential to each other, without one the other cannot create experience. his theory was devised to answer the question "is philosophy possible", as in is the fundamental question of philosophy solvable, being:

- how do we know things?
- how do we overcome sceptical challenges with such knowledge?
- does the world exist separately of our experience
- can we justify causality, induction and existence in the self

Kant believed answering these were essential if we came to justify claims about the good in a moral sense, including the existence of god, freedom of will and the nature of the soul. Kant's solution is it is possible, as the world as experience it is predicated on constructs of the mind. he is saying that both empiricists and rationalists are right, empiricists are right that experience is needed to make knowledge, but rationalists are right that concepts are needed for the world to make any sense. but this only applies to the "phenomenal world", the world as we see it, we cannot ascertain such things for the world as it is in itself. to do this we need to circumvent having an experience of it in order to observe, which would make it the observed world instead of the unobserved. Kant calls the world in itself noumenal reality, and says the only certainty is it differs from phenomenal reality. the problem is, moral questions lie in the noumenal world, so does that mean we cannot know or comment on such things? and where exactly does such the boundary lie between the phenomenal and noumenal reality, what are the limits of reason. Kant calls an enquiry into such a limit a "transcendental enquiry", investigating what boundary conditions exist for experience and knowledge to be possible. the conclusion to his inquiry was the previous claim that we cannot know beyond the boundary of experience. this means discussion of morals and god's existence need to be undertook differently than they have before, being considered as question on par with ones that can be known.

the first Critique is made of 2 parts, the first Kant lies out his scheme of how experience arises. we sense what we do via sensory modalities, the senses then interpreted by the faculty of understanding via it's general concepts, such as causality, substance, quality and quantity. Kant shows that without such concepts experience is in essence impossible, making them the vital concepts for experience. the second part called the "Dialectic of Pure Reason" examines what happens when reason tries to go beyond the bounds of viable thought, showing how we cannot prove the existence of god or an immortal soul, or free will, instead arguing that we have to make assumptions on the answers to such questions, but should never assert to know answers. like Hume before him Kant was primary concerned with the foundations of morality, and thus the first Critique despite establishing important concepts for metaphysics and theory of knowledge is only a starting point. his following works, the two most important being *Critique of Practical Reason* and the *Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals* Kant sets out his austere view of the "supreme principle of morality."

if such a thing as morality exists, it's laws apply only to the realm of free will, not the realm of nature. this means that the task of moral philosophy is to show how pure reason governs and directs the will. the will thus when truly autonomous expresses the highest good there can be, being freedom. free willed beings says Kant should be treated as "ends in themselves". to Kant moral value comes from actions taken from a sense of duty, especially the duty to obey the moral law which reason recognises to be the right one for the circumstances. the law will not take the form of a "hypothetical imperative", which Kant describes as an argument that advises an agent should do so and so if they wish to achieve a certain end, but a "categorical imperative", which only asserts to do so and so, it being unconditional. Kant arrives at this view by distinguishing between subjective action-guiding principles and objective principles which apply to all rational beings. he calls subjective principles "maxims" and objective principles "laws". perfectly rational beings only act according to laws, and animals never do due to lacking reason. Human's exist at an intermediary point, having both reason but also subjective appetites and instincts. thus they alone need concepts of duty and obligation in order to act morally. Kant generalises the categorical imperative as "I ought never to act in any way other than according to a maxim which I can at the same time will should become a universal law" circumventing our inability to truly know of such universal laws by supposing we should only assume our most willed maxims to be the solution.

Kant championed science and many times criticised religion, but despite that did not think it possible to justify morality without the concept of a deity or the soul, as well as free will. this makes a trinity of concepts that are unprovable but are useful to assume, as it creates a sense of being liable for our actions after life. this in his opinion is what gives people their sense of morals.

### Philosophy in the the Nineteenth Century
the 19th century is where the modern definition of "philosopher" is found. it saw the rise of 2 strands come into focus. the work of Hume and Locke expanded by Bentham, Mill and the American pragmatists expanding their epistemology, ethics, law and politics. there is then Hegel, Schopenhauer and the British idealists expanding metaphysical frameworks, which seemed identical to the other group until Nietzsche, that turned it to a "continental" school of thought focusing on sociology, history and criticism of literature. the separation becomes cemented in Husserl and Heidegger.
#### Bentham
Bentham was known much more on the continent than his homeland of England owing to his works appealing to only experts of law, government, sociology, ethics and economics, but took of with his most influential works being only published in translations into French, then Russian, Spanish and German during his lifetime, only appearing in English a decade after his death. he also was a political radical republican, putting him in opposition to the establishment of England, so thus received little recognition or honour. yet he introduced into England the words utilitarian, codify and international, the first to suggest an international court of arbitration for peace keeping and international law, and many prisons and hospitals follow his panopticon model. Bentham first studied the works of Hume, Helvétius and Bonesana-Beccaria, finding in each respectfully the idea of utility being criteria of virtue, the idea utility can guide conduct by ideas of pleasure and pain, and the concept of the promoting the greatest happiness of the greatest number. he denoted utility to mean things that produce benefits, advantages, goodness or happiness. utilitarianism whether his kind or the later more elaborate one is a consequentialist theory of morality, evaluating moral value only by the outcomes of actions, leaving aside intentions or personal characters that precede them. they consider every persons individual happiness equal, meaning morals should work to produce the greatest happiness in the greatest number of people regardless of who they are.

Bentham thought pleasure and pain to be the governing factor in what we do, say and think, the view known as hedonism. the problem is that hedonism doesn't necessarily promote a course of action that algins with the cultivation of the greatest happiness in the greatest number of people. Bentham tried to circumvent this by saying personal happiness is cultivated by helping others, or to moderate the hedonistic view slightly and say sometimes actions are done out of benevolence, this being called "enlightened self interest", but the problem is that we sometimes exceed helping others and go to lengths that disadvantage us, and that it is still underpinned by a school of thought that is ultimately self serving. Bentham rejected Hume's moral psychology, considering that ideas of character are too subjective to be useful. only individuals can know what their intentions are, and reason for such actions are opaque, making judgements of right and wrong hard. Pressure by his patron the first Marquis of Lansdowne to address the question of achieving perpetual peace by an international order lead him to coin the term "international" and a the idea of a court for international disputes. he was especially interested in the revolution of France, writing a series of pamphlets before the revolution suggesting what solutions and reform could be put in place. his support of the revolution lead him to gaining honorary French citizenship in 1792.

he considered law to be a negative doctrine, suggesting restrictions or limits reducing liberty, it being "negative liberty". combined with his doctrine of pleasure and pain, it is pleasurable to be free and painful to be chained, thus liberty is good and restriction bad. but he did not consider liberty a right, considering no rights natural. he thought the social contract to be false, thinking that people always existed in society and laws only the commands of the authority. this anticipates, legal positivism proposed by John Austin later in the 19th century. his key problem was the influence exercised by individuals or claques from the King downwards on politics, acting through unrepresentative parliaments who were in their pockets. he advocated for political reform to combat this. he argued for extension of enfranchisement, annual secret ballot elections, basic qualifications needed to stand for parliament, a system of fines to enforce attendance by MP, accurate reporting of debates and abolishing the royal patronage in offices and honours. he also believed in female suffrage but only once all adult males had it first. he also argued expenditure needed to be reduced and the efficacy of civil service increased by selecting and training civil servants better. he believed in religious liberty, arguing against discrimination on religious grounds such as baring from education or government. 
#### Hegel
Hegel's first celebrated work *The Phenomenology of Spirit* was completed around the victory of Napoleon and the battle of Jena, publishing it in a magazine before becoming a headmaster at a school.  he received a salaried university position in 1816, where he wrote the *Encyclopaedia of the Philosophical Sciences in Outline* and 1821 *Philosophy of Right*. Hegel saw philosophy as a part of political and social reform, thinking it a necessary response to divisions in society, particularly as a means of restoring the harmony between ideas and practice. they criticised the disarray of German culture of the time and considered Kant the only thing to be proud of in German culture, and even then are critical of his philosophy. saying he is akin to Moses leading the people out of exile but they still need to guide themselves to the Promised Land. his earliest work being a collaboration with his friends is *The Oldest System-Programme of German Idealism*, outlining an early version of Hegelian Philosophy. it begins with the Kantian idea of individuals being radically free moral beings, where freedom is subject to laws that we impose on ourselves. but it then expands it saying the reason can devise and self apply such laws as a sign that it is not limited to what Kant discusses, instead reason can transcend the boundary of empirical experience and giving an answer to the question "What must the world be like for a moral being". it is answered in terms of a metaphysics of ideas. the authors clearly think science would benefit from being released by the constraint of what they describe "plodding" experimental procedure. but more importantly it provides a whole new theory of state through ideas. this is done by saying there is no 'idea" of the state, as ideas have elements of freedom while the state is simply a mechanical apparatus like a machine. they thus propose to transcend the state to free people from being treated as parts of such a machine. the programme thus provides the basis for this new kind of society in which all are equal and free because they have liberated and educated themselves to allow autonomy.

by the time he wrote *The Phenomenology of Spirit* his revolutionary beginnings had subsided, as he came to think Philosophy's purpose was to spread the idea that the dialectical journey of Geist had reached fulfilment. the word "Geist" means mind or spirit, and for Hegel is a universal mind or agency that all human mind's participate in. Hegel think's history to be the story of the development of Geist, it coming to greater and greater self0realization and fulfilment. Hegel thought this development had reached it's apex, and the authoritarian monarchical Prussian state was the embodiment of this apex. he considers the development of Geist to lead to greater freedom by having absolute knowledge, calling such knowledge the "knowledge of the Absolute" which is achieved as we develop mere sensory knowledge through increasing degrees of self-consciousness. Hegel made a particular point that as the self conscious requires, consciousness of others, it is felt most strongly through a desire for what is not itself, another consciousness, and for possessions of things in it's environment. in other words, desire for changing and appropriating the world or aspects of it. this greatly interested Marx who developed it into a school of thought that wished to not just understand the world but change it.

the most celebrated part of the Phenomenology is the discussion of the relationship between master and slave, and the claim that the natural expression of recognition of other consciousness is not peace but conflict, but the soon realisation that this would lead to destruction causing one side to give way, creating an asymmetrical master-slave relationship. but this relationship is unstable, the master considers the slave an object, so the slave seeks acceptance by working and creating, but such creations are not owned by the slave but the master, the slaves production being stolen. this idea inspired Marx to develop his theory of alienation of labour, slaves (or workers) have their products of labour taken from them, denying self affirmation and oppressed due to this. Hegel thought stoicism was an attempt to escape such alienation, by making the fruits of labour inner strength and development of the mind, making your circumstance indifferent to being a master or a slave. but such detachment is flawed to Hegel and only leads to an "unhappy consciousness" or the soul to become alienated, where master and slave become discordantly combined in 1 consciousness. the consciousness seeks to be freed from the material but is unable to truly transcend, causing it to suffer. Hegel targets Christianity or other religions that also try to turn human nature against itself. he sees the alienation of the soul the end product of suggesting a god that exists apart from humans and the world exists. saying a spiritual side that is higher exists leads people to wish to transcend to it, only leading to internal conflict.

Geist solves this by gaining self-awareness of the absolute knowledge of reality in itself and facilitating full freedom. Hegel calls this outlook "absolute idealism", saying that ultimate reality is in itself mental. less developed consciousness does not realise this, thinking reality independent of itself, and that perception and reason are tools to discover aspects of such an independent reality. the failure of such tools to grasp reality is what lead Kant to think that they only operate as the world appears to us, being largely governed by how the mind configures the appearance. but once the mind learns all reality to be constructed by itself, it stagnates as there is nothing to know beyond itself. absolute knowledge is thus "mind knowing itself as mind". in essence Hegel says Geist has reached it's apex cause Hegel himself thinks to have understood the nature of reality. as Hegel describes his position as "absolute idealism" opposed to "subjective idealism", you might think Geist to be a universal mind, perhaps even god. but it could not be a conventional god as until Hegel came along, it would not have been fully aware of itself and the nature of reality, and Hegel emphasised the induvial mind to be understood socially and in fact the universal mind is shared between all people together. people act in accordance once they set aside selfishness and act in line with the cosmic consciousness. Hegel uses the dialectic to facilitate this. truth is approached, the opposing arguments clashing and creating a more refined truth, which happens over and over again. Hegel thought the dialectic explained not just problems or concepts but the entire history of the world, as the history of the Geist was developed by such dialectical clashes. a thesis meet sit's antithesis and the confrontation emerges a synthesis. the final state of Geist marks the final synthesis of the historical dialectical chain. but in this frame work he seems to say India and China are "outside of history" having not contributed to the dialectical development. he thus considers Persia the starting point of "true history" due to being the first great empire to fall, leading into the Greek city states, into Rome then pre-reformation Catholicism, until the reformation traces the Geist into Germany.

Hegel was a constitutional monarchist, did not think the people should vote, and thought freedom of expression shou8ld be limited. to him the state was the ideal of the divine and should thus be worshiped.
#### Schopenhauer
in 1818, Schopenhauer published his principal work, *The World as Will and Representation*. in it his theory is Kantian, accepting the ideas of the Phenomenal and Noumenal worlds being separate and structure coming by how we experience and conceptualize it. he differs however on how we access the noumenal, saying it to be possible, being experienced as will. will predates conscious knowledge, and is separate from it. knowledge is secondary to it, will underpins everything as a fundamental thing, everything only being an appearance of it. Schopenhauer's will is blind, a relentless expression of itself, manifesting as desires of man, leading to suffering at their lack of fruition. to be free of suffering you must release yourself from will, from desire itself. this has a contrast with Buddhist thought, Schopenhauer noting his affinity for Buddhism and incorporating it into his views. a temporary respite is found in contemplation of art and music, allowing us to combine ourselves and the representations of the world. music is the most potent transcendence of suffering, being the most pure, universal and timeless art. this view of suffering also grounds his ethics, being based in compassion for others and animals. he specifically says compassion to be participation in the suffering of others with no ulterior motives to prevent or eliminate it, any other act being not of moral worth. his point in reflection is that other system of morality boil down to egotism, religion wishes to please a deity to be rewarded, and despite agreeing with Kant that morality should entail treating people as ends and not means, he disagrees in treating the agent and patient as separate things in the moral act. *The World as Will and Representation* also has the first serious philosophical discussion of sex, influencing Darwin and Freud in his theory of sexual desire. it also expresses a relaxed approach to homosexuality. he was liberal in politics, agreed with Plato on eugenics and expressed women as of "more sober judgement" and compassionate than men, but nonetheless suited for nurses or teachers due to being "childish, frivolous and short-sighted". later in life his degradation of women diminished.
#### Positivism
Positivism in the 19th and 20th centuries described many different outlooks, so it's worth clarifying the various meanings of it. the most famous is Logical Positivism from the 20th century, which is described later. the 19th saw Auguste Comte and legal theory Positivism. in it he says the answer to the question "what is a valid law?" is it was enacted by a proper authority or derived from precedent, which means laws have nothing to do with justice or morality, meaning not it's merits. bad laws are still valid as long as proper authorities enact them. there is also the Positivism of Comte, who invented the term itself. to him Positivism is the view that knowledge only comes from empirical scientific sources, being organized experience of phenomena based on logic. it rejects metaphysics and theology as sources of knowledge. Come thought that Galileo, Bacon, Descartes and Newton would agree with him, and were the true founders of the discipline. he thought that the laws of behaviour in individuals were as scientifically derived as natural science, leading to his theory of positivism to become a near cult called the religion of humanity.
#### Mill
Mill is primarily remembered for 2 short works, *On Liberty* and *Utilitarianism*. *On Liberty* seeks to answer what limit of power society should have over individuals. Mill says society should not dictate what individuals do concerning things that only apply to themselves, and should only interfere when an individuals actions will harm others. this means individuals should be free unless a good reason exists as to why they should not be. he is considered a "classical liberal" for this, but still thought authoritarian government in "backward states of society" to be legitimate. he invites difficulties by imply we can harm others by what we do, but also what we fail to, implying we are free to live our lives as see fit as long as such a life fits into the criteria of doing what you should do. regarding utilitarianism, he advanced Bentham's doctrine to include "higher and lower pleasures", and a good judge should know both types first-hand. of course such "higher pleasures" are those Mill considers good.

#### Marx
Marx produced his primary works between 1843 and 1848 in Paris, including *Economic and Philosophical Manuscripts* and *Theses on Feuerbach*, his work culminating in *Capital*. his students mark his book cowrote with Engels *The German Ideology* as the moment he broken from the Young Hegelians and philosophical idealism, instead asserting a concept of historical progress governed by material conditions. it preludes the most famous Marx-Engels work *The Communist Manifesto*. mark was focused on discussing commodity production, markets, division of labour, distinguishing use-value and exchange value and the labour theory of value that underlies it, surplus value and related such topics.. critics identify flaws in his technical case, but his analysis of tension between worker and capitalist continue to be persuasive. Marx offers a leftist critique of liberalism. in his essay *On the Jewish Question*, he criticizes the view that religion is an obstacle to human emancipation, he thought liberalism advanced over more repressive forms of society, but itself a barrier to the true emancipation of people, placing protective walls between people that stops them coming together. the perfect state needs no such provisions, as harmony will prevail people moving to the common cause. this view is utopian, leading to the rightful observation why some may take their chances with liberalism. to achieve such a revolution, history must be understood materially to see how people have been politically and economically oppressed, allowing people to break free. he thought such progress was dialectical, thinking the contradiction between the material interests of different classes to become conflicts, which evolve over time into the end state of communism where the state "whither away".
#### Nietzsche
Nietzsche was primarily concerned with ethics, wishing to understand it from a perspective of antiquity, namely to answer questions what kind of person is good, what life is good and what values should guide us. Nietzsche thought the west followed the wrong answers to such questions, thinking this distortion informed by Judaeo-Christian morals. he also criticised other philosophers who accepted such morals but then argued for independent justification for it that did not require such theistic grounding. his declaration "god is dead" was an assertion that foundations of theism and morals had collapsed, and if such a fundamental basis was gone for culture we would need to create our own new values to base ourselves on. his sister Elisabeth took some of his passages after his death and misread them to produce sinister interpretations for the Nazis. compared with Schopenhauer his theory of the Uber mensch becomes clear although.
#### Idealism
In the second half of the 19th century British and American idealists began to appear, principally T. H. Green, F. H. Bradley, J. M. E. McTaggart, James Ward, Bernard Bosanquet and Josiah Royce. They didn't share the same outlines but all for specific reasons espoused idealism. Be it a reaction to the preceding two centuries of empiricism and utilitarianism, or reacting to advancements in biblical scholarship, geology, evolution and increased literacy, or being influenced by Hegel. Green postulated an eternal consciousness described in 2 unfoldings, through evolution of an individual will, and an unfolding of the increasing or actualization of god. He described eternal consciousness to be the law of nature or god, or its idea. He phrases it this way to insinuate that it is strongest when people properly understand it is within them, and actualize it in themselves. It evolves by god making himself gradually more manifest

Individuals must however further understand, Green argued, that each of them contains only a spark of the eternal consciousness, and that the full realization of the divine in the world requires cooperation and communality. The ‘self’ of an individual exists as part of a society of selves, and individual self-realization is a contribution to the self-realization of all. His social theory built upon this, wishing for everyone to fulfill their freest free will by existing in the most satisfying state of self, but recognizes that working together means accepting a common good and shared laws. Societies are formed by individuals, and individuals formed by society. This makes the state on the local and national level important as it affects every individual and allows them to effect the state, but the state should work to only uphold rights and duties that best lead to people's self-actualization.

Bradley's metaphysical system at the time was influential, but largely in how Moore and Russell pushed against it, leading to the emergence of 20th century Analytic Philosophy. He argued that academic discussion of the world by philosophers and scientists contained internal contradictions that only appear when our experiences are tried to be placed into a system. In order to address this, he argues that pluralism and realism must be disregarded, leading us to think of reality only as a complete whole, called the Absolute. Like Hegel who argues the Absolute to be made of Spirit, Bradley argues it to consist of experience or sentience.

McTaggart argued that reality is spiritual and timeless, and consists of a community of mutually loving spirits. To him, time either consists in ordering events, or indicating what has happened, what is happening or will happen. He calls the later "A-series" and the former, "B-series". Arguing for time to be real then both series need to hold and be consistent. But the A-series holds a contradiction, in that any given event from different frames of reference exists in all states, of past, present or future as at some point it has not yet happened, at one is happening, and another already has happened. As it is arbitrary as to what label should be assigned to the event in the series, It's impossible to give it a single label that is preferable or knowingly correct.  This puts into question of the A series exists in theory, and makes talking of the past present of future meaningless, and time a shaky concept that may not even exist.
#### Pragmatism
In the last decades of the 19th century, American philosophers Charles Sander Peirce, William James, and later John Dewey, formed a philosophical outlook called Pragmatism. in Peirce's most influential writing *How to Make our Ideas Clear,* Pierce outlines Pragmatism to be defined by the maxim ‘consider what effects, which might conceivably have practical bearings, we conceive the object of our conception to have. Then, our conception of these effects is the whole of our conception of the object.’ this states our ideas of things have a basis in what we can practically extract from it. This is strictly regarding meaning, not truth, truth by extension being attained when sciences eliminates ideas of no practical worth, settling them only on those of utility.

James sought to address an issue of this definition, namely that this implies there is some secondary force directing truth, which inquirers then converge on. He did this by instead focusing on the effectiveness of belief, saying true is only what is the most expedient way of thinking, just as right is only the expedient way of acting this has the obvious objection that there are useful falsehoods, and truths that are not useful, so this has no guarantee of truth.

Dewey instead approaches Pierce's philosophy by describing it as beginning on unsettled terms and aiming to transform it into a coherent one. The inadequate starting truth, begins not only in inadequate beliefs but also an objectively unsettled nature of the situation they exist in. Dewey explicitly states belief to not exist in a vacuum, all starting with some pre-existing bias. It also implies skepticism to not be possible, as it does not disregard beliefs but rather be optimistic any set of beliefs can be evolved into a correct one by convergence.
