# Summary
Socrates and Euthyphro, both outside the court of Athens, discuss Piety and in extension, Justice. Socrates being brought to be prosecuted for corrupting the youth asks Euthyphro to explain what is pious and impious, who is prosecuting his father for the death of a slave. Euthyphro cites Zeus, who punished and castrated Cronus for eating Zeus' siblings, analogous to the murder of the slave. Socrates first refutes the "slander of the gods", but retorts to Euthyphro that this isn't adequate for what is pious, but an example of piety. Euthyphro then goes on to say what is dear to the gods is pious, and what is not is impious. 

Socrates is more delighted by this answer, but upon inspection decides it still to have a fault. the gods have disagreements just like men do, so in this framework there is no *common* idea of what is dear to the gods. each god has different values, and thus, would see different things as pious. in fact some gods hate what others love, making them simultaneously pious and impious. Euthyphro agrees with this all, but responds that no man, or god, would think murder is just, and thus murder must atleast be impious. Socrates retorts that yes, they would all agree on that, but would disagree or fight in the event of murder *who* is the murderer.

Socrates returns to the main topic, asking Euthyphro to prove all gods would believe his act of prosecuting his father for murder is just, as he himself is not necessarily pious for attempting to bring his father to justice for an impious action. they follow that some actions are pious, some impious and some neither or both. so what does this act fall under? and for that matter, if the pious is things loved by gods, is it pious because the gods love it, or do the gods love it because it is pious? through logical deduction, Socrates extracts from Euthyphro a peculiar definition

*to be god-loved is to be loved by the gods, but what is pious is loved by the gods for being pious. these are not the same thing and thus, something being loved by the gods is not grounds to be called pious.*

they move on then to equating piety and justice. in a similar vein, Socrates asks if all that is just pious, or is all that is pious just? which set contains the other and more? they agree that the pious must be part of the just, but what part? Euthyphro respond the pious must be what actions care for the gods, while the rest of justice concerned with the care of men. Socrates refutes this, saying this definition defines piety as whatever is of benefit to the gods like a stableboy cares for a horse, as a lesser. Euthyphro clarifies he doesn't not mean this, but like how a slave cares for a master. Socrates asks then, what is the primary goal the gods aim to achieve, as that would be the root of what is pious.

Euthyphro responds that the primary care we give is prayer and sacrifice, to ask for things from them and to give to them like in trade. Socrates then asks what the gods could need from us? what do we give them that is of use in the same way what we ask from them is of use to us? Euthyphro responds we give them honour, reverence and please them. Socrates then concludes it's what pleases them, not what is beneficial or dear to them that is pious. Euthyphro responds it's all dear to them. they in essence return to where the line of questioning started, having not moved at all in circular logic. Euthyphro hurries off, saying Socrates has wasted enough of his time.
# General notes
jumping slightly ahead in philosophy but the entire conversation about deferring to what the gods love is reminiscent of [[Friedrich Nietzsche]] and his principle that there is no such thing as good and evil, as it's all subjective.
