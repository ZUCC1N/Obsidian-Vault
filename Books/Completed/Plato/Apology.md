# Summary
Following [[Euthyphro]], Socrates attends his trial for impiety. upon his charges, Socrates begins by accusing his prosecutors of persuasion and denying the charge of it laid against him, saying he can only persuade of the truth, and asks to be given grace for his rather frank way of speaking. he goes on to say there are more accusers than that directly went against him, and says they also don't even believe in the gods despite laying charges against him for that act. he theorises this could be due to not being a man of science, and that he is not a teacher nor takes levy for teaching. he chastises the sophists for inflating their idea of knowledge and then asks why is it that he can be called wise and evil for the same thing? the oracle of Delphi said Socrates to be the wisest man, which puzzled Socrates who held he knew nothing. he went to politicians, poets, artisans and asked them of their crafts but was disappointed in their results. he thus concluded that god was not saying in literal terms he was wise, but that men who know they know little in the grand scheme are the wisest of all. this pursuit but took up much of his time, driving him to poverty and made him many enemies. he says these to be the first class of his accusers.

Socrates then turns to his prosecutor Meletus, he asks him who improve the young, slowly extending Meletus' answer to all Athenians besides Socrates. he says then that good men are better than bad men, and he would never wish to intentionally corrupt them. he therefore should not be prosecuted, but only redirected in his teachings. despite saying him to deny the gods and invent his own, Meletus also says Socrates to be an atheist, Socrates retorting that he is being mistaken for Anaxagoras, for such antiquated claims such as thinking the moon of earth and sun stone.

He has been a soldier, and has remained at his post as he was ordered. now his calling is philosophy, and it would be shameful to desert it. he would rather die than that. he contents that the greater evil here is the act of trying to rob a man of life for doing what he should, and he wishes not to think his judges evil. Socrates is a messenger from god in his service, and they would be unable to rest soundly if they killed him due to that. he says he tries to avoid politics as an honest man cannot survive long, but was dragged into it when he resisted the democracy and the 30 tyrants incriminating him by association. among the crowd at the trial are many of his pupils, but none have been called to justify the charges of their corruption, but also that he would not stoop so low to call upon them for emotional appeal as that only embarrasses both him and the state. he is seeking to convince them, not ask favour of them.

after the verdict of his punishment, Socrates goes on to make a final speech. he says that in the hour of death men are given prophetic power, and that his accusers will face a far greater punishment upon his death. if they think by killing someone they can censor their evil they are mistaken, as the easiest way is to improve others, not drag them down. he says death is simply a passing on, where he will speak to other men who suffered death unjustly, and he will never stop searching for knowledge, as it is said he will be immortal there:

<p style="text-align: center;"><i>
 'The hour of departure has arrived, and we go our ways—I to die, and you to live. Which is better God only knows.'<br>
- Socrates (470 BCE - 399 BCE)
</i></p>
