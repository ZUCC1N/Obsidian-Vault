## Biography
a younger colleague of [[Anaximander]], Anaximenes learned from both him and [[Thales]]. He agreed with Anaximander that the [[arche]] is infinite, but agreed with Thales in thinking that it was material, being *aer*, or a sort of dense moist air.
<p style="text-align: center;"><i>
‘Anaximenes … like Anaximander declares that the underlying nature is one and unlimited but not indeterminate, as Anaximander held, but definite, saying that it is air. It differs in rarity and density according to the substances it becomes. Becoming finer, it turns to fire; when condensed, it comes to be wind, then cloud; and when further condensed, it becomes water, then earth, then stones, and the rest come to be from these. He makes motion eternal, and says that change also comes to be through the eternal motion of the air.’ 
 <br>
Theophrastus (371-287 BCE)
</i></p>
Anaximenes held that the earth is flat and sits on top of air. Earthquakes occur when the earth is either too dry or too wet, for when too dry it cracks, when too wet it falls apart. The sun, moon and stars are flat fires but are too distant for us to feel their heat. The sun does not circle underneath the earth but instead rides round the circumference of earth, night is simply the time where the sun is on the other side of the earth. Anaximenes is notable as his arche accounts for *how* it constitutes different substances, unlike Thales, being heat and density:
<p style="text-align: center;"><i>
‘As Anaximenes of old believed, let us leave neither the cold nor the hot in the category of substance, but as common attributes of matter, which come as the results of its changes. For he declares that the contracted state of matter and the condensed state is cold, whereas what is fine and ‘loose’ (calling it this way with this very word) is hot. As a result he claimed that it is not said unreasonably that a person releases both hot and cold from his mouth. For the breath becomes cold when compressed and condensed by the lips, and when the mouth is relaxed, the escaping breath becomes warm because of rareness.’ 
 <br>
Plutarch (610-546 BCE)
</i></p>
